# A Taxonomy of Mental Health and Technology Needs for Alzheimer's and Dementia Caregivers

**Date:** 2026-06-19
**URL:** http://arxiv.org/abs/2606.19247v1

**Abstract/Summary:**
This research addresses the significant mental health challenges faced by family members caring for individuals with Alzheimer's disease and related dementias (AD/ADRD). These caregivers, often termed "invisible second patients," experience elevated rates of mental health problems, yet existing research frequently oversimplifies their complex psychosocial experiences into a single construct of caregiver burden. This obscuration prevents a clear understanding of specific unmet needs or effectively supported areas.

The study acknowledges the rapid expansion of digital and AI-enabled technologies, from smartphone applications and videoconferencing to sensor platforms and AI chatbots, as potential tools for support. However, it highlights a critical absence of shared frameworks across medicine, psychology, and technology research, which impedes cumulative progress in this domain.

To bridge this gap, the authors introduce a **Caregiver Mental Health and Technology Taxonomy**. This taxonomy systematically links the identified needs of AD/ADRD caregivers with corresponding classes of technology-based interventions. The development of this framework is based on an interdisciplinary literature review combined with insights from two qualitative studies conducted directly with caregivers.

Key findings and contributions of the taxonomy include:
*   Identifying significant mismatches between caregivers' priorities and the types of technological support currently available.
*   Highlighting under-served mental health domains, such as relational strain and compassion fatigue, which are critical yet often overlooked by current technological solutions.
*   Proposing concrete design directions for the development of more adaptive and responsive technology systems, particularly AI-enabled ones, that are better tailored to caregivers' dynamic needs.

The proposed framework is intended to serve as a shared vocabulary, offering guidance to clinicians, researchers, and technology designers. Its ultimate goal is to facilitate the development of more person-centered and clinically grounded innovations within dementia care, thereby improving the mental health and well-being of AD/ADRD caregivers.
