# AI usage patterns are shaped by perceived gains in human agency

**Authors:** Not specified in the provided text
**Date:** 2026-07-03 (based on URL v1)

## Summary:

This paper investigates the psychological underpinnings of sustained conversational AI usage, moving beyond traditional trust-based models. Through an ethnographic study of 51 daily AI chatbot users across the US, Germany, and Singapore, the research demonstrates that users consistently link continued AI engagement with their perceived gains in individual agency.

Key findings include:

*   **Agency as a primary driver:** Perceived increases in individual agency are the most significant factor shaping AI usage patterns, often outweighing concerns about accuracy, reliability, or consistency.
*   **Challenge to trust models:** Existing models of human-AI interaction that rely heavily on trust are insufficient to explain the long-term behavioral patterns observed with conversational AI.
*   **Sociotechnical practice:** AI usage is viewed as a situated sociotechnical practice, deeply embedded in users' daily lives.
*   **Critical tension:** A notable tension exists where immediate psychological boosts to perceived agency might not necessarily translate into material effects, structural empowerment, or sustained long-term capacity.

The study advocates for new behavioral frameworks, measurement tools, and AI benchmarks that focus on strengthening human agency in a substantial and lasting manner, rather than solely on user satisfaction or task completion.
