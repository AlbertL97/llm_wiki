"Ask three models" (LLM-council) is the first, simplest version of a synthetic cognitive system. But there's more to that.  
  
Let's have a look at how this can evolve:  
  
1) LLM-council (variance as signal).  
  
One model gives you a story. A council gives you distribution: what’s invariant across models vs. what is model-specific style, ideology, training-data artifact, and so on. The point is spotting where the space of plausible answers is wide, where it collapses, and where you’re being steered by a single convincing narrative.  
  
2) Modeling the user.  
  
When you run a council, pretty soon you learn a painful fact: the same "best practice" advice can be wrong for this person because of constraints the models don’t see (professional context but also some psychological traits like risk tolerance, energy, incentives, ethical red lines, appetite for ambiguity). A useful system has an explicit user model: goals, constraints, preferences, and failure modes.  
  
3) Matching "expert" to the user’s situation.  
  
Generic experts are noisy. What you want is a fit between expert stance and personal situation. Even in context of business, sometimes quadruple espresso advice from virtual "Alex Hormozi" isn't going to be helpful. The system has to become a curated advisory board.  
  
4) Protocols for debiasing (because models are persuasion engines).  
  
LLMs produce clean narratives, and narrative is inherently convincing. So the system must include routines for questioning output: structured counter-arguments, forced uncertainty, explicit assumptions, reference-class checks, premortems, or "what would change your mind?" prompts. As with debiasing strategies in healthcare - you don’t rely on willpower to resist being convinced, but on checklists and procedures.  
  
5) Protocols for injecting intuition as data (not as truth).  
  
Most high-quality human judgment starts as a weak signal: a somatic "no," a vague discomfort, an image, a half-sentence. If you don’t capture it, it never enters the system. The right move is to treat intuition as input features: capture (notes/voice), translate into hypotheses/constraints, and then ask the council to test it. I used to teach people how to enter hypnagogic state on demand (it's not a woo, but a pretty well researched brain state that doesn't require drugs) as a way to increase access to those weak signals.  
  
Most decision makers aren’t even at level 1. They’re still treating a single model’s fluent answer as "the answer," when it’s usually just "a plausible story told confidently."
