AI-Avatars Versus Human-Avatars: A Comparative Study of UserInteraction Behaviors in Live StreamingKai Qiao, Xiaoping Sheng and Zhenyu LiSchool of Cultural Heritage and Information Management, Shanghai University, Shanghai, ChinaABSTRACTThe rapid advancement of artificial intelligence (AI), especially large language models,has made human-computer interaction more dynamic and personalized, therebyaccelerating the development of AI-driven virtual humans (AI-Avatars). Based on theComputers Are Social Actors (CASA) paradigm, this study employs text mining andexperimental methods to compare user interactions in live streaming contexts withHuman-Avatars and AI-Avatars. The findings indicate that curiosity about “AI” acts as aprimary factor in user engagement. In AI-Avatar contexts, users’ danmaku are morediverse and information-seeking, whereas in Human-Avatar contexts, they are moreuniform and socially oriented. Despite these differences, users show similar interactionpatterns across both types, highlighting the functional viability of AI-Avatars. However,limited social intelligence prevents AI-Avatars from building interaction ritual chainsand emotional resonance, limiting long-term user engagement. These findings enrichour understanding of the psychological and social dynamics underlying human inter-actions with AI agents in mediated environments.KEYWORDSAI virtual humans; largelanguage models; human-computer interaction;Computers Are SocialActors; interaction ritualchain1. IntroductionWith advancements in science and technology, the concept of “virtual” is increasingly becoming the “newreal”. The rapid development of virtual humans exemplifies this transformation. As digital representationsof humans, avatars, which are commonly known as virtual humans, are powered by deep learning andother computational algorithms and are now widely utilized across multiple industries. They serve as vir-tual influencers in brand marketing, customer service agents, and streamers in live streaming contexts(Munnukka et al., 2022; Nukhu et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2025). Businesses favor avatars for their loweroperational costs, increased availability, and reduced reputational and behavioral risks (Allal-Cherif et al.,2024). However, the widespread adoption of avatars remains constrained by technological challenges, par-ticularly the complexity of character design and the lack of natural interactions. The emergence of largelanguage models (LLMs) presents new opportunities to enhance avatars’ interactive capabilities, as theirprecise word prediction and robust intent recognition enable more coherent and human-like dialogues(Shoa et al., 2023). This increased anthropomorphism, a key attribute of avatars that enhances social pres-ence and promotes consumer behavior (Munnukka et al., 2022), may also induce technological anxiety,which can negatively affect consumer perception (Xiao et al., 2025).Artificial intelligence (AI) reshaping the notion of anthropomorphism in avatars. Currently, mostvirtual influencers are digitally rendered Human-Avatars operated by human teams. These avatars buildemotional bonds with users by blurring the virtual-real divide (Mei, 2021). In contrast, AI-driven vir-tual humans (AI-Avatars), which operate autonomously through AI technologies, are gaining attention.The advent of AI-generated content (AIGC) has accelerated interactions between users and non-humanagents, including service robots and virtual assistants (Angmo et al., 2025). Understanding users’ motivations (Gerlich, 2023) and interaction patterns (Lou et al., 2023) is crucialfor effectively designing and developing AI-Avatars. The Computers Are Social Actors (CASA) para-digm provides a robust theoretical framework, suggesting that humans naturally apply social normswhen interacting with computers (Reeves & Nass, 1996). This perspective highlights the relevance ofresearch on live human streamers and human-like avatars, which we refer to as Human-Avatars. In livestreaming environments, common practices such as danmaku and tipping function as rich channels forsocial interaction, conveying user motivations, purchase intentions, and emotional expressions (Xionget al., 2024). Through danmaku, emotional expressions can accumulate into collective emotional energy,enhancing both user experience and engagement. According to interaction ritual chains (IRCs) theory,such interactions can be understood as collective rituals that generate shared emotional energy andstrengthen social bonds. A key question is whether AI-Avatars can elicit comparable social and emo-tional responses within the CASA framework, thereby realizing their commercial potential.Research indicates that AI-Avatars enhance user engagement and show advantages in monetizedinteractions (Zhang et al., 2025). They are especially effective substitutes for human streamers inutilitarian scenarios (Yuan et al., 2025). Jin and Viswanathan (2024) found that users’ perceptions ofidentity threats and the personalization of AI-Avatars are key to adoption. To explore these dynam-ics, researchers often employ text mining and empirical methods. For instance, Nukhu et al. (2024)introduced a text-mining approach to explore Reddit users’ perceptions of AI-Avatars. While Changet al. (2025) employed both methods to conduct a comparative analysis of AI- and Human-Avatars,finding that users’ desire for promotional content was the primary driver of their engagement withAI-Avatars. Still, current research mostly focuses on user attitudes. Subtle interaction behaviors, suchas expressive traits and interactional motivations, remain underexplored, especially regarding theirimpact on user feedback and responses across avatar types. Despite the promise of AI-Avatars, par-ticularly those integrated with LLMs, important knowledge gaps remain in understanding their inter-action dynamics.However, its application to avatars lacks clarity on whether these interactions follow human–com-puter or interpersonal logic. This study applies the CASA framework and investigates user–avatar inter-action patterns to evaluate its relevance in the context of AI-Avatars. We pose three research questionsto address current gaps:RQ1: What are the primary interaction focuses of users when engaging with AI-Avatars compared toHuman-Avatars?RQ2: How do user interaction behaviors differ between AI-Avatars and Human-Avatars?RQ3: How do users’ social connections change when interacting with AI-Avatars versus Human-Avatars?This study combines in-depth text-mining of danmaku with empirical methods to validate our findings.Guided by the CASA framework, our comparative analysis of AI-Avatars and Human-Avatars yields keyinsights. Unlike previous studies that have primarily focused on user attitudes or purchase intentions, thisstudy adopts a novel perspective by linking real-time behavioral patterns to the underlying psychologicalmechanisms in AI-Avatar interactions. Through systematic analysis of user behaviors, it provides actionableinsights for improving the design and functionality of AI-driven virtual agents. As AI technologies reshapethe landscape of human–computer interaction (HCI), understanding user engagement through real-timebehavioral analysis becomes essential for capturing how such technologies are adopted and experienced ineveryday contexts. By employing multiple empirical methods to analyze live streaming danmaku and user–avatar engagement, this study advances research in the field of human–AI interaction. It highlights notablebehavioral differences between interactions with AI-Avatars and Human-Avatars, thereby offering newinsights into how emerging technologies shape user behavior and social experience.2. Literature review2.1. AI-AvatarsTraditionally, avatars have been defined as computer-generated entities that emulate human appearanceand behavior (Burden & Savin-Baden, 2019). However, the concept has diversified into multiple2 K. QIAO ET AL.

subtypes, including virtual influencers, employees, anchors, teachers, idols, and assistants (Zhang,Zhang, et al., 2024). Each role fulfills unique functions; for instance, virtual influencers are crafted bybrands and interact directly with users like human influencers (Stein et al., 2024). These avatars areoften designed using advanced computer graphics and are supported by human teams who carefullymanage their public persona and communication strategies to foster emotional engagement. Some evenappear in real-life imagery alongside humans, further blurring virtual-real boundaries (Arsenyan &Mirowska, 2021). Notable examples such as Lil Miquela and Lu do Magalu illustrate this trend. Virtualassistants, on the other hand, are primarily functional – deployed across sectors like finance (Priya &Sharma, 2023), retail (Kamoonpuri & Sengar, 2023), and healthcare (Ali, 2023). Yet companies increas-ingly endow them with names and visual identities to humanize their presence.Technological advancements, particularly LLMs and multimodal systems, have further enabled ava-tars to process natural language and interact across diverse modalities (Wang, Yuan, et al., 2024). Forexample, AI-Avatars such as Neuro-sama now leverage AIGC to develop algorithmically defined per-sonalities, facilitating affective interactions with users (Li & Pang, 2024). Their low cost, operationalflexibility, and reputational neutrality make them an attractive option for organizations, while usersvalue their perceived trustworthiness and storytelling capabilities (Allal-Cherif et al., 2024). However,sustaining engagement relies heavily on delivering pleasurable user experiences and eliciting positiveuser attitudes (Li et al., 2023).Research highlights agency as a critical factor in differentiating interaction experiences. Park andSung (2023) found that users responded more favorably to fully automated AI-Avatars compared toHuman-Avatars. The former were seen as more engaging, credible, and effective in eliciting interactionintentions. Yet, much of the literature has failed to distinguish between content generated by AI andcontent curated by human teams, especially in studies on anthropomorphism. As the line betweenhuman-like expression and machine-generated content continues to blur, this distinction becomesincreasingly significant.Growing attention has thus shifted to the study of AI-Avatars. Prior research has primarily focusedon how the inherent attributes of AI influence user responses. For example, Munnukka et al. (2022)demonstrated in a service context that perceived anthropomorphism, social presence, dialogue richness,and attitudinal cues positively shaped users’ evaluations of AI-Avatars. Similarly, interaction patternshave been shown to vary across user segments (Blut et al., 2024). Park and Sung (2023) further foundthat abstract or stylized AI-Avatars generated different perceptions of attractiveness and credibility thanhuman-like designs and simultaneously reduced the uncanny valley effect (Pavlidou, 2021). Existingstudies largely rely on self-reported surveys to examine isolated factors shaping user perceptions ofindividual AI-Avatars. While informative, these approaches provide limited insight into how users actu-ally interact with AI-Avatars in real contexts. Comparative research with human streamers is thusessential. For example, Chang et al. (2025) found that users engaging with AI-Avatars were morestrongly influenced by promotional information than by Human-Avatars. Yet, this line of work primar-ily offers implications for commercial applications rather than addressing the intrinsic attributes of AI-Avatars, which constrains its capacity to inform how such avatars might realize their full potential.Building on these findings, the present study investigates human-like AI-Avatars and their agency char-acteristics to advance understanding of user interaction behaviors.2.2.
