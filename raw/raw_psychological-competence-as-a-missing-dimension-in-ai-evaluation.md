## Summary

Current AI evaluation frameworks predominantly focus on technical performance metrics such as accuracy, robustness, reasoning ability, and policy compliance. While these are essential, they are inadequate for AI systems that engage directly with users via natural language, particularly in roles like advisors, coaches, tutors, and companions. The interactions of these AI systems can profoundly shape user cognition, emotional interpretation, belief formation, trust calibration, and decision-making processes. Consequently, the evaluation should shift to encompass the human-AI interaction itself, not just the model in isolation.

The paper introduces 'psychological competence' as a vital, yet overlooked, dimension for evaluating human-facing AI. This competence is defined as the AI system's capability to support user cognition, emotional interpretation, and behavioral decision-making in a manner that is appropriate to the user, the context, and the interaction's purpose. Key interaction properties contributing to psychological competence include framing, tone, perceived authority, responsiveness, uncertainty handling, and conversational guidance.

Existing evaluation methods capture fragments of these psychological effects but rarely assess them directly. The authors propose a conceptual framework for psychological competence and its core domains, drawing on behavioral science and human-AI interaction research. Instead of a specific benchmark, they focus on defining the construct, delineating its boundaries, and outlining assessment methodologies. These methods include scenario-based probes, structured human evaluation, and model-assisted evaluation techniques.

The paper advocates for psychological competence to be a primary consideration for AI model providers, deploying organizations, researchers, and regulators, emphasizing its importance for understanding and managing the real-world impact of human-facing AI systems.

## Key Findings:

*   **Inadequacy of Current AI Evaluation:** Technical performance metrics are insufficient for human-facing AI systems.
*   **Introduction of Psychological Competence:** A new evaluation dimension is proposed to assess AI's impact on users' psychological states and processes.
*   **Definition of Psychological Competence:** The capacity of AI to appropriately support user cognition, emotional interpretation, and decision-making.
*   **Key Interaction Properties:** Framing, tone, perceived authority, responsiveness, uncertainty handling, and conversational guidance are critical.
*   **Proposed Assessment Methods:** Scenario-based probes, structured human evaluation, and model-assisted evaluation.
*   **Call for Integration:** Psychological competence should be a core consideration for all stakeholders involved with human-facing AI.
