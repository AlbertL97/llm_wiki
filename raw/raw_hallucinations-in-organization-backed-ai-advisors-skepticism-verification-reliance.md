Summary:
This paper investigates user behavior when interacting with organization-backed generative AI advisors that can produce inaccurate information (hallucinations).

Key questions addressed:
- Do users rely on inaccurate AI information?
- Do users recognize when AI responses require verification?

Key constructs distinguished:
- Skepticism: User doubt about the AI's information.
- Verification: The act of checking the AI's information.
- Reliance: The extent to which users depend on the AI's information.

Findings across various domains (product search, medical decision-making, content generation, chatbot tasks):
- Reliance is frequently measured, but user skepticism and verification are often targeted by interventions rather than measured directly.
- Cues prompting scrutiny are predominantly related to the AI output (e.g., source citations).
- Organizational interventions like general or specific warnings about hallucinations show weak and mixed effects.
- The question of whether users scrutinize responses more in certain content areas remains open, as no studies varied content category.

Implications for future research:
- Measuring skepticism and verification separately from reliance is crucial for a clearer understanding of user behavior.
- Further exploration is needed to identify effective methods for promoting user verification and managing reliance on potentially inaccurate AI outputs.
