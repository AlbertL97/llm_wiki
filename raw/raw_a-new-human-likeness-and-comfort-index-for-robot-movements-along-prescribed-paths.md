# A New Human-Likeness and Comfort Index for Robot Movements Along Prescribed Paths

## Abstract/Summary:

As human-robot interaction (HRI) becomes more prevalent across various domains, robot acceptance is a critical factor. The study argues that visual resemblance alone, as seen in humanoid robots, is insufficient for ensuring acceptance in physical interactions. Instead, acceptance is closely tied to perceived comfort and ergonomics, which are influenced by the quality of robot movements as observed by humans.

This research establishes a connection between perceived comfort and the similarity of robot movements to human movements. By analyzing the kinematic characteristics of human motion, particularly focusing on the temporal aspects (time laws) of movements with prescribed end-effector paths, the paper proposes a 'human-likeness index'. This index is derived from the lognormality principle, commonly used to model human movements.

The defined index allows for an a priori evaluation of trajectory generation algorithms, enabling researchers to assess their ability to produce human-like movements before physical execution. The study's validation involved 68 subjects who were asked to rate their comfort levels. Three experimental campaigns involving direct physical interaction with a robot demonstrated a consistent correlation between subjects' preferences for comfort and the distribution of the proposed human-likeness index.

## Key Findings:

*   Visual similarity to humans is not sufficient for robot acceptance in physical interaction; perceived comfort and ergonomics are more critical.
*   The quality of a robot's movement, specifically its similarity to human movement patterns, directly impacts perceived comfort.
*   A novel 'human-likeness index' is developed, based on the lognormality principle of human motion, to quantify the human-likeness of robot trajectories.
*   This index can predict the perceived human-likeness and comfort of robot movements prior to their execution.
*   Experimental validation with 68 subjects confirmed a positive correlation between the human-likeness index and perceived comfort/preference in physical HRI.

## Methodology:

*   Kinematic characterization of human movement, focusing on time laws of movements with prescribed paths.
*   Application of the lognormality principle for modeling human movements.
*   Definition and application of a 'human-likeness index'.
*   Experimental validation with 68 subjects rating comfort during physical interaction with a robot.

## Implications:

*   Provides a quantitative metric for evaluating and improving robot movement design for better human acceptance.
*   Offers a tool for trajectory generation algorithms to optimize for human comfort and likability.
*   Highlights the importance of dynamic, kinesthetic aspects of robot behavior in HRI.
