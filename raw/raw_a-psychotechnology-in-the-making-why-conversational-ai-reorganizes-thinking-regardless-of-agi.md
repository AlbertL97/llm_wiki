Description

Whether or not artificial general intelligence is near, a different question about conversational AI is already pressing: has it begun to reorganize how thinking is done? What makes conversational AI distinctive is not its intelligence but its medium. Conversation is the medium of self-directed thought, and conversational AI is the first cognitive technology whose output is format-identical to it, returned as addressed, contingent language. To make the claim testable, this paper proposes four criteria for when a practice counts as a psychotechnology in this demanding, literacy-class sense: it must be repeated; it must redistribute cognitive labor; it must become socially transmitted, institutionally stabilized, or embedded in shared defaults; and some of its effects must outlast a single episode of use. Calibrated against literacy, calculators, consumer GPS, and web search, the criteria make web search the critical comparator: it already meets much of the threshold as a psychotechnology of retrieval and transactive memory, so conversational AI cannot be distinctive merely because it offloads information work. What is new is the medium, which can accelerate trust, make repair feel like consultation, blur source monitoring, and turn defaults into roles. Applied to current evidence, the verdict is uneven: studies support immediate influence, redistributed work during use, and some bounded persistence. Two claims should therefore be kept apart.
