## Abstract

### Background

Artificial intelligence-based follow-up systems offer efficient solutions for postdischarge care but face limited patient acceptance due to trust concerns. Trust is a key determinant of acceptance, yet existing instruments are insufficient for assessing trust in artificial intelligence-driven follow-up contexts. This study focused on developing and psychometrically validating a scale to assess patient trust in artificial intelligence-based follow-up.

### Methods

This methodological study was carried out in three sequential stages: item generation, scale development, and psychometric validation. During the initial phase, a preliminary item pool was established based on theoretical frameworks, existing literature, semistructured interviews, and focus group discussions. In the second phase, a preliminary scale was developed using the Delphi method, content validity assessment, and a pilot survey. In the third phase, a questionnaire was administered to patients who had experienced artificial intelligence-based follow-up. The structure was first determined by exploratory factor analysis (EFA), refined via item analysis, and then validated using confirmatory factor analysis (CFA). Reliability was assessed by computing internal consistency (Cronbach’s α, McDonald’s ω) and test–retest correlations.

### Results

The final scale comprised 27 items across three dimensions: patient dispositional trust, system interaction trust, and environmental trust. Content validity was high (I-CVI: 0.833–1.000; S-CVI/Ave: 0.955). EFA revealed three factors that explained 66.0% of the variance. CFA indicated a good model fit (χ²/df = 1.096, RMSEA = 0.022, CFI = 0.999, TLI = 0.99). The reliability indices were strong (Cronbach’s α = 0.931; McDonald’s ω = 0.960; test–retest = 0.901).

### Conclusion

The Artificial Intelligence-Based Follow-Up Trust Scale demonstrated strong reliability and validity. This valuable tool can assess trust in artificial intelligence-based follow-up systems, supporting future research and the development of strategies to increase trust and improve follow-up adherence.

**Keywords:** Artificial intelligence, Follow-up, Trust, Scale development

## Introduction

Follow-up care is an essential component of patient management that enables healthcare providers to monitor disease progression and provide necessary guidance for rehabilitation \[[^1]\]. Traditional follow-up methods, such as telephone calls, home visits, and outpatient appointments, are labor intensive and time consuming and often fail to meet the increasing demands of patient care \[[^2]\]. With the growing emphasis on improved recovery protocols, reduced hospital stays, and the expansion of day-case surgeries, a significant number of patients are discharged while still in the recovery phase, and timely follow-up is needed to ensure optimal health outcomes \[[^3]\]. However, the limited availability of healthcare professionals poses a major challenge to the implementation of large-scale follow-up, particularly in regions with constrained medical resources. Recent advancements in artificial intelligence have introduced new opportunities for improving follow-up efficiency \[[^4], [^5]\]. Artificial intelligence-powered follow-up systems utilize big data analytics, deep learning algorithms, speech synthesis, speech recognition, and natural language processing to automate follow-up procedures. Compared with conventional follow-ups, artificial intelligence systems can engage simultaneously with multiple patients, which significantly increases efficiency while maintaining comparable accuracy in data collection \[[^6], [^7]\]. Furthermore, artificial intelligence-driven follow-up systems alleviate clinician workload by automating routine tasks, thereby enabling healthcare providers to dedicate greater attention to complex patient care and specialized interventions.

Despite these advantages, the widespread adoption of artificial intelligence-based follow-up remains challenging, largely due to issues of patient acceptance and trust. In the field of artificial intelligence, trust is conceptualized as a user’s psychological confidence that an artificial intelligence system can reliably achieve a specific goal in uncertain or vulnerable situations \[[^8]\], is a key determinant of acceptance and engagement. Studies indicate that although many patients are aware of artificial intelligence technologies, persistent concerns include its ability to offer personalized recommendations, communicate effectively, and safeguard data privacy \[[^9]\]. A cross-sectional study further revealed that over 50% of participants feared artificial intelligence might reduce face-to-face interaction with physicians, while others expressed doubts regarding its adaptability, real-time responsiveness, and the transparency of its decision-making processes \[[^10]\]. Such apprehensions—including unease about the “black-box” nature of algorithms and overall system predictability—contribute to patient skepticism and reluctance toward artificial intelligence-based follow-up \[[^11]\].

Empirical evidence suggests that a lack of trust in artificial intelligence-based follow-up services may lead to lower patient engagement, interruptions in postdischarge care, reduced adherence to follow-up recommendations, and increased hospital readmission rates. Given the pivotal role of trust in artificial intelligence adoption, accurately assessing patients’ trust in artificial intelligence-based systems is crucial for optimizing these technologies and improving patient experiences. However, most existing artificial intelligence trust scales have been developed for specific contexts such as robotics and automated systems \[[^12], [^13]\], or exhibit limitations in psychometric properties. For example, the human-robot interaction trust scale developed by Yagoda et al. \[[^14]\]was based on a small sample size, lacked reported reliability metrics, and must be used in conjunction with Rotter’s Interpersonal Trust Scale \[[^15]\], which limits its practicality as a standalone tool. Although the social service robot interaction trust scale introduced by Chi et al. \[[^13]\]demonstrated good internal consistency (Cronbach’s α = 0.71–0.93), it was developed using scenario-based simulations rather than actual interaction experiences, and thus requires further validation through field studies. Overall, existing instruments still fail to adequately capture key factors influencing trust in artificial intelligence within healthcare settings.

There is therefore a critical need for a validated and reliable instrument that is specifically designed to assess patient trust in artificial intelligence-based follow-up systems. Such a scale can provide a standardized measure of trust levels, facilitate early interventions to increase acceptance of artificial intelligence, and contribute to the refinement of artificial intelligence-driven healthcare solutions. The purpose of this research was to develop and validate an artificial intelligence-based follow-up trust scale that is reliable and applicable in clinical practice.

### Theoretical framework

This study is grounded in Zucker’s trust source theory, which identifies the determinants of trust—referred to as trust sources—as the foundational conditions under which trust can emerge. Gaining an understanding of these sources enables a more comprehensive assessment of trust and increases the validity of trust measurements. According to this theory, the trust source model comprises three components: (1) a trustee-based model, which emphasizes perceived trustworthiness; (2) a trustor-based model, which focuses on the individual propensity for trust; and (3) an institution-based model, which reflects trust shaped by social norms and environmental structures \[[^16]\]. The characteristics of the trustor (i.e., the person placing trust) are key determinants of trust from the user’s perspective. The formation of trust is significantly influenced by personal attributes, such as personality traits, life history, cultural background, and prior experiences, all of which contribute to an individual’s general propensity to trust. Conversely, the trust source associated with the trustee (i.e., the system being trusted) is derived primarily from its perceived trustworthiness. According to Mayer et al. \[[^17]\], perceived trustworthiness is determined by three core dimensions: ability, benevolence, and integrity. Sztompka et al. \[[^18]\] expanded this model to include appearance, performance, and reputation as additional indicators of perceived trustworthiness. In real-world contexts, the development of trust involves not only the trustor and the trustee but also external elements that influence the trust dynamic. These additional influences are collectively referred to as the trust environment. Zucker’s theory has been widely applied in research on physician–patient trust and the trust mechanisms of online healthcare platforms, and its utility as a comprehensive and effective framework for understanding and measuring trust has been demonstrated \[[^19]\].

Unlike general human-machine trust contexts where physical safety or industrial efficiency may dominate, artificial intelligence-based trust must account for patients’ health vulnerability, absence of direct human supervision, and concerns about clinical accuracy in automated assessments. The dispositional dimension captures patients’ pre-existing attitudes toward technology-enabled care; the interaction dimension assesses perceptions of the artificial intelligence’s technical competence and communication quality; while the environmental dimension reflects confidence in the healthcare institution’s oversight and data protection measures.

### Phase I: item generation

#### Literature review

We performed a systematic literature review utilizing controlled vocabulary terms and free-text keywords across major biomedical databases in both Chinese and English. The Chinese-language search encompassed CNKI, Wanfang, CMB, and VIP databases, while the English-language search included PubMed, EMBASE, the Cochrane Library, and Web of Science. The search terms included “patient,” “follow-up/return visit/continued nursing/continuing care/after care/transitional care/post-hospital follow-up,” “artificial intelligence/robots/intelligent voice/interactive voice,” and “trust.” The search period covered all records from the inception of each database to June 2024. The inclusion criteria were as follows: (1) studies focused on artificial intelligence-based follow-up care or trust in artificial intelligence and (2) publications in either English or Chinese. The exclusion criteria were as follows: (1) duplicate publications; (2) unavailable full texts; and (3) conference abstracts or review articles. The systematic search identified 5,563 potentially relevant articles. Following rigorous screening and eligibility assessment, 43 studies met all inclusion criteria and were retained for final analysis, comprising 25 Chinese-language publications and 18 English-language publications.

#### Semistructured interviews

We employed purposive sampling to select participants for semistructured telephone interviews. The study population consisted of patients who had utilized artificial intelligence-enabled follow-up services at a tertiary care hospital in Nanchang, Jiangxi Province, during the first quarter of 2024 (January to March). The principle of maximum variation sampling was applied to ensure diversity in the patients’ disease types, age, gender, educational background, place of residence, and history of telephone fraud experiences. The interviewer was the researcher, who had previously been trained in the theoretical knowledge of qualitative research. Each interview lasted approximately 20–40 min and was conducted based on a pre-designed interview outline that contained open-ended questions designed to explore patients’ perceptions, experiences, and concerns related to trust in artificial intelligence-based follow-up. The interviews were discontinued once data saturation was achieved \[[^20]\]. Participants met the following inclusion criteria: (1) adult patients (≥ 18 years) discharged from the hospital who had completed at least one cycle of artificial intelligence-based follow-up care; (2) willing and able to provide informed consent for voluntary participation in all study procedures; and (3) the ability to communicate effectively. Participants were excluded based on the following criteria: (1) patients with severe complications or comorbidities and patients who were in end-stage conditions or deceased and (2) patients with a history of psychiatric illness or severe cognitive impairment. Participants who withdrew from the study midway were excluded from the final analysis. A pilot interview was conducted with three patients to finalize the interview guide, which included the following questions: (1) What did you know about artificial intelligence-based follow-up services before using them? (2) What impact do you think the artificial intelligence-based had on you? (3) Do you perceive any risks associated with artificial intelligence-based follow-up? (4) Before you used the service, what factors influenced your willingness to trust and use artificial intelligence-based follow-up? (5) When you used artificial intelligence-based follow-up services, what aspects affected your trust in them? (6) What is your overall impression of the artificial intelligence-based follow-up experience? (7) Whose attitudes and recommendations influence your trust in artificial intelligence-based follow-up and why? (8) What suggestions do you have for improving trust and experience with artificial intelligence-based follow-up? All interviews were audio-recorded in full. The data were analyzed using Colaizzi’s seven-step method. Within 24 h after each interview, the interviewer and a second graduate student transcribed the recordings into Word documents, incorporating nonverbal cues such as tone and pauses noted during the interview \[[^21]\]. In cases of disagreement during data analysis, group discussions were held to reach consensus on the final themes. A total of 15 patients were interviewed, resulting in the identification of three main themes and eight subthemes.

#### Group discussion

A comprehensive set of 56 initial items was developed by synthesizing pertinent information derived from the 43 selected articles, in addition to integrating insights gained from the semistructured interviews. Subsequently, these items underwent a thorough evaluation, modification, and deliberation by the research team. Through a series of iterative group discussions, the pool of items was distilled down to 42 distinct items, which were organized into three preliminary dimensions: patient dispositional trust, system interaction trust, and environmental trust.

### Phase II: scale development

#### Expert consultation

Between June and August 2024, questionnaires for expert consultation were disseminated to specialists through email and WeChat. Each round of consultation lasted for one month, with an interval of 2 to 3 weeks between rounds. The recommended number of experts for a Delphi consultation is between 15 and 50, and 2 to 4 rounds are typically conducted \[[^22]\]. In this study, 18 experts were selected. The inclusion criteria for the experts were as follows: (1) professionals in the fields of medicine, psychology, or informatics, with expertise in clinical medicine, clinical nursing, nursing informatics, artificial intelligence, or medical big data; (2) at least 10 years of experience in their respective field; (3) for clinical nursing experts, a bachelor’s degree or above was required, while other experts were required to have a postgraduate degree; and (4) provided informed consent and participated voluntarily. Experts who withdrew from the study or failed to return the consultation form in a timely manner were excluded. In the first round, experts were provided with the initial pool of items and asked to rate each item using a five-point Likert scale (1 = strongly disagree; 5 = strongly agree), as well as to provide open-ended comments on modifications, deletions, or additions to the items. At the end of each round, two researchers independently entered and summarized the experts’ feedback. The following metrics were calculated: response rate, item mean score, coefficient of variation (CV), expert authority coefficient (Cr), and Kendall’s coefficient of concordance (W). A response rate ≥ 70% indicated a high level of expert engagement; Cr ≥ 0.7 was considered indicative of high expert authority, and a higher Kendall’s W with *P* < 0.05 indicated better agreement among experts \[[^23]\]. Items were screened using the following criteria: items with a mean score < 3.5 were removed, and items with CV > 0.25 were reviewed for potential deletion or revision. Furthermore, the research team deliberated on the recommendations made by the experts concerning the modifications of items and dimensions, integrating them as deemed suitable \[[^24]\].

#### Content validity

A total of six specialists from the second round of expert consultations were chosen to appraise the pertinence of each item using a four-point scale, with ratings ranging from “very relevant” (4) to “very irrelevant” (1). In order to evaluate the content validity of the scale, two specific indices were computed: the item-level content validity index (I-CVI) and the average content validity index for the scale (S-CVI/Ave). The I-CVI was defined as the proportion of experts who rated an item as either 3 or 4 divided by the total number of experts. The S-CVI/Ave was calculated as the average of all I-CVI values across the scale items. According to widely accepted standards, an I-CVI ≥ 0.78 and an S-CVI/Ave ≥ 0.90 indicate acceptable content validity \[[^25]\].

#### Pilot survey

In September 2024, a pilot survey was conducted using convenience sampling. Ten patients from a tertiary care facility in Nanchang were chosen based on established criteria for reliability and validity testing. Their selection aimed to assess the clarity, relevance, and time required to complete the items and response options of the scale. On the basis of participant feedback, it was noted that the term “artificial intelligence-based follow-up” should be defined more clearly in terms of its format. Additionally, patients were generally more familiar with the term “return visit” than with “follow-up,” which led to some confusion. Accordingly, revisions were made to address the most frequently reported issues from the pilot test. The findings revealed that the mean time required to complete the scale was 11.56 ± 2.24 min. Following these adjustments, a preliminary version of the scale was finalized that consisted of 33 items across three dimensions.

### Phase III: psychometric testing of the scale

#### Participants and sample size

From September to December 2024, a convenience sampling method was employed to recruit patients. To ensure the sample encompassed a range of clinical contexts, recruitment was conducted from three key departments (internal medicine, surgery, and gynecology) of a tertiary hospital in Nanchang. This approach, often referred to as stratified convenience sampling, utilized departments as strata to guide participant recruitment, while actual sampling within each department remained non-probabilistic and based on availability. The criteria for inclusion comprised: (1) individuals aged 18 years or older who had been discharged from medical care and subsequently utilized artificial intelligence-based follow-up services; (2) patients who granted informed consent and willingly opted to engage in the complete survey procedure; and (3) patients who had the ability to communicate effectively with the artificial intelligence-based follow-up system. The exclusion criteria were as follows: (1) patients with severe complications or comorbidities, those with terminal illness, or those who were deceased and (2) individuals with a history of psychiatric disorders or severe cognitive impairment. The elimination criteria were as follows: (1) incomplete questionnaires or participants who withdrew during the survey process and (2) questionnaires displaying patterned or uniform responses across all items (e.g., wave-like or identical answers). The necessary sample size for evaluating the reliability and validity of a scale largely hinges on the approach employed for the assessment of validity. In this study, factor analysis was employed, including both EFA and CFA. For EFA, it is generally advised that the sample size should be between five to ten times the total number of items assessed \[[^26]\], so at least 165 participants were required. CFA generally requires a minimum sample size of 200 participants \[[^27]\]. Taking into account a possible 10% to 15% rate of invalid responses, the objective was to collect a minimum of 406 questionnaires. In the end, a total of 420 questionnaires were gathered, out of which 397 were considered valid and subsequently divided into two random samples, see Table [2](#Tab2) for details of participants. Sample 1, which comprised 190 participants, was utilized for conducting EFA. In contrast, Sample 2, consisting of 207 participants, was employed for CFA, item analysis, and reliability assessments.

#### Survey instrument

Based on the initial scale, a questionnaire was designed that consisted of the following components: (1) instructions for completing the questionnaire; (2) a general information survey; and (3) the initial Artificial Intelligence-Based Follow-Up Trust Scale. The scale included three dimensions and a total of 33 items. A 5-point Likert scale was employed for all items, with response options spanning from strongly disagree (1 point) to strongly agree (5 points). Notably, items 11 through 13 were scored in reverse. A higher total score signified a greater degree of trust in the follow-up provided by artificial intelligence.

### Validity testing

#### Construct validity

The evaluation of construct validity was conducted through the application of both EFA and CFA. To ascertain the appropriateness of the data for factor analysis, the Kaiser‒Meyer‒Olkin (KMO) measure along with Bartlett’s test of sphericity were employed. A KMO value exceeding 0.6 \[[^28]\] and a statistically significant outcome from Bartlett’s test (*P* < 0.05) signified that the data were suitable for factor analysis. Common factors were identified using principal axis factoring with promax rotation, retaining only those with eigenvalues exceeding 1. To objectively determine the optimal number of factors to retain, parallel analysis was conducted by generating 1,000 random datasets with parameters derived from our own data. Items were retained if they demonstrated factor loadings above the 0.4 threshold on their respective components \[[^29]\]. Items with cross-loadings—i.e., those that loaded significantly on multiple factors with similar values—were considered for deletion to ensure the clarity of the factor structure. CFA was conducted using R software version 4.3.1 to assess the model fit of the final scale. The following fit indices were used to evaluate model adequacy: a chi-square to degrees of freedom ratio (χ²/df) under 3 indicated good model fit; a root mean square error of approximation (RMSEA) under 0.08 indicated acceptable fit; and values over 0.90 for Comparative Fit Index and Tucker‒Lewis Index (TLI) suggested acceptable model fit \[[^27]\].

#### Convergent and discriminant validity

Convergent validity pertains to the extent to which items that measure the same construct demonstrate a high degree of correlation. This validity was evaluated using two key metrics: the average variance extracted (AVE) and composite reliability (CR). Generally, an AVE exceeding 0.50 alongside a CR greater than 0.70 is regarded as indicative of strong convergent validity \[[^30]\]. Discriminant validity was assessed by comparing the square root of the AVE for each factor with the correlations between that particular factor and the other constructs. It is deemed that discriminant validity is acceptable when the square root of the AVE for each construct surpasses its correlation with any other construct \[[^31]\].

#### Item analysis

The selection of items was performed utilizing two distinct approaches: (1) The Pearson correlation coefficient was employed to determine the relationship between individual item scores and the overall scale score. Any items yielding a correlation coefficient of *r* < 0.40 and a p value exceeding 0.05 were subsequently excluded from the analysis \[[^32]\]. (2) A measure of internal consistency, commonly referred to as the Cronbach’s α method, was utilized to assess whether the exclusion of any particular item would enhance the overall Cronbach’s α coefficient of the scale. If so, the item has low homogeneity with other items and therefore may not be suitable for retention. Corrected item‒total correlations (CITC) were also considered. Generally, items with CITC values above 0.5 are preferred, values between 0.3 and 0.5 are acceptable, whereas items with CITC < 0.3 are recommended for deletion due to insufficient correlation with the overall scale \[[^33]\].

#### Reliability assessment

Assessment of the scale’s reliability was conducted through examination of its internal consistency as well as test–retest reliability. (1) Internal consistency reliability: The evaluation of internal consistency employed Cronbach’s α coefficient and McDonald’s ω \[[^34]\]. A Cronbach’s α coefficient and McDonald’s ω exceeding 0.70 signify an acceptable level of internal consistency, while a coefficient greater than 0.80 suggests good reliability. Furthermore, a coefficient surpassing 0.90 reflects an excellent degree of reliability \[[^35]\]. (2) Test–retest reliability: To assess the temporal stability of the scale, a second administration was conducted 2 to 4 weeks after the initial survey using a subsample of 10% of the original participants. A total of 20 patients were surveyed again. The consistency between individual items and the total scale score was assessed using the Intraclass Correlation Coefficient (ICC), ICC < 0.5: poor reliability; 0.5 ≤ ICC < 0.75: medium reliability; 0.75 ≤ ICC < 0.9: good reliability; ICC ≥ 0.9: excellent reliability \[[^36]\].

#### Data collection

Data were gathered through a blend of both online and offline techniques. The offline method targeted follow-up patients from outpatient clinics and inpatient departments. The researchers distributed the questionnaires after physicians completed their ward rounds and during patients’ post-lunch rest periods. Prior to data collection, the researchers introduced themselves and explained the purpose and significance of the study. Written informed consent was obtained before the patients were assisted in completing the questionnaire. For the online method, eligible participants were screened via the hospital’s artificial intelligence-based follow-up system. After informed verbal approval was gained from the participants by telephone, the researchers conducted the questionnaire interview by reading each item and recording the responses. Standardized training was given to all researchers to ensure uniformity in the administration procedure.

#### Statistical analysis

Data entry was performed using Excel 2021. EFA was conducted based on a polychoric correlation matrix using the psych package (version 2.3.6) in R (version 4.3.1), employing Promax rotation. The extraction method used was Principal Axis Factoring (PAF). Parallel analysis was performed to determine the optimal number of factors to retain. Using parameters from our dataset, we generated 1,000 random datasets and identified appropriate eigenvalues via parallel analysis. Based on the EFA results, CFA and reliability analysis were carried out using the Weighted Least Squares Mean and Variance Adjusted (WLSMV) estimator from the lavaan package (version 0.6–20) in R (version 4.3.1). Item analysis was conducted using SPSS (version 26.0). Experts’ demographic characteristics, authority, and engagement level were summarized using descriptive statistics. Item mean scores, coefficients of variation, and Kendall’s coefficient of concordance were calculated based on expert ratings. Normally distributed continuous variables are presented as mean ± standard deviation, while categorical variables are expressed as frequencies and proportions.

#### Content validity

The results of this study showed that the I-CVI for each item ranged from 0.833 to 1.000 and the S-CVI/Ave was 0.955. These findings indicate that the scale demonstrated good content validity. Thus, the scale development phase culminated in an initial scale (Table S1) with 3 dimensions and 33 items. The three dimensions extracted are named based on Zucker’s theory of sources of trust to ensure conceptual consistency: (1) patient dispositional trust, reflecting the patient’s own tendency to trust the technology; (2) system interaction trust, which is concerned with perceptions of the artificial intelligence system’s capabilities and the quality of interactions; and (3) environmental trust, which involves confidence in the external environment such as institutional safeguards and data security.

#### Item analysis

A total of 207 valid questionnaires that were used for item analysis were integrated in Sample 2. (1) Correlation coefficient method: Correlation coefficients between individual items and the total scale score ranged from 0.41 to 0.73, all entries greater than 0.4 and p values < 0.05. The overall Cronbach’s α value did not change after deleting any of the items, indicating that the items did not reduce internal consistency. Based on the above results, we retained all items.

#### CFA

CFA was performed using 207 valid questionnaires from Sample 2. To validate the factor structure, we conducted a validation factor analysis using the lavaan package for R (version: 0.6–20). In this analysis, we designated all topics as ordinal categorical variables and used WLSMV as the estimation method to obtain accurate parameter estimates and model fit indices, as shown in Fig. [2](#Fig2). The CFA results indicated a χ²/df value of 1.096, which is less than 2, and an RMSEA of 0.022, which is less than 0.05, both suggesting good model fit. Other fit indices also supported the model’s adequacy, i.e., CFI = 0.999, TLI = 0.999, all of which exceeded the acceptable threshold of 0.90, this indicates a good model fit. The standardized factor loadings for the items ranged from 0.583 to 0.920, all of which were above the recommended minimum value of 0.50, indicating that each item loaded well onto its respective factor.

## Discussion

### The scientific validity of the Artificial Intelligence-Based Follow-Up Trust Scale

This study developed and validated the first known scale specifically designed to measure patient trust in artificial intelligence-based follow-up systems, rigorously grounded in Zucker’s trust source theory. The systematic development process—encompassing an extensive literature review, in-depth qualitative interviews with patients, and iterative expert consultations—ensured the scale’s strong theoretical foundation and content validity. The high response rates and authority coefficients from the expert panels further affirm the reliability of the scale development process. The resultant three-dimensional structure (patient dispositional trust, system interaction trust, and environmental trust) effectively captures the multifaceted nature of trust, aligning with the theoretical framework that trust arises from the trustor, the trustee, and the institutional environment \[[^19], [^37], [^38]\]. This comprehensive approach ensures that the scale is well-positioned to serve as a valid and authentic tool for assessing trust in this emerging healthcare context.

### The Artificial Intelligence-Based Follow-Up Trust Scale demonstrates good reliability and validity

The psychometric evaluations confirmed that the Artificial Intelligence-Based Follow-Up Trust Scale is a robust measurement instrument. The scale demonstrated a clear and stable three-factor structure, which was empirically supported by both EFA and CFA. The model fit indices from the CFA indicated an excellent fit to the hypothesized structure, validating the conceptual distinction between the three dimensions \[[^39]\]. Furthermore, the scale exhibited strong internal consistency and excellent test-retest reliability, indicating that it produces stable and coherent measurements over time. However, the stratified Cronbach’s α coefficient of 0.613, while acceptable, was relatively low. This might indicate that the scale dimensions measure relatively independent constructs, with limited inter-factor correlations—a pattern often observed in multidimensional scales designed to capture distinct yet related aspects of a broader construct. The establishment of good convergent and discriminant validity confirms that the scale accurately measures the intended construct of trust and that its dimensions are related yet distinct. These comprehensive psychometric properties underscore the scale’s readiness for application in research and clinical settings.

### The Artificial Intelligence-Based Follow-Up Trust Scale demonstrates clinical practicality

The development of this scale has significant practical implications for enhancing artificial intelligence-facilitated care. In clinical practice, the scale provides a standardized metric to identify patients with low trust levels, enabling healthcare professionals to deliver targeted education and support to improve engagement. For system developers, the subscale scores offer actionable, granular feedback. For instance, consistently low scores in “System Interaction Trust” could pinpoint issues with voice recognition accuracy or communication quality, while low “Environmental Trust” scores may signal concerns about data privacy, guiding iterative refinements to align systems with user expectations \[[^40]\]. By facilitating a quantitative understanding of trust barriers, this scale can inform the creation of strategic interventions, support policymaking for trustworthy artificial intelligence adoption, and ultimately contribute to improving patient adherence and the overall effectiveness of artificial intelligence-based follow-up systems \[[^41]\]. Notably, the widespread adoption of artificial intelligence-based follow-up systems still faces significant challenges, especially in regards to factors such as ethical concerns, data privacy, and emergency responsiveness. Data security is a paramount concern that directly impacts the “Environmental Trust” dimension. Patients need robust assurances that their sensitive health information is protected from unauthorized access within automated systems. Future research using this scale should investigate how specific security measures (e.g., transparent data anonymization policies) quantitatively influence trust scores. Algorithmic transparency problem poses a direct challenge to “System Interaction Trust.” A lack of clarity on how artificial intelligence reaches its conclusions can erode trust, particularly if a recommendation seems counterintuitive. Integrating explainable artificial intelligence principles into follow-up systems and studying their impact on trust scores should be a priority. Finally, equity in artificial intelligence adoption must be addressed to prevent the exacerbation of existing health disparities \[[^42]\]. This scale can serve as an audit tool to probe for algorithmic bias; by analyzing trust scores across different demographic groups, researchers can identify if certain populations are systematically less trusting, thereby promoting more equitable artificial intelligence implementation and design.

### Score interpretation and practical guidelines

To enhance the practical utility of the Artificial Intelligence-Based Follow-Up Trust Scale, we provide clear guidance on score interpretation. The scale consists of 27 items rated on a 5-point Likert scale. The total score ranges from 27 to 135, with higher scores indicating a greater level of trust. The subscale scores are calculated as follows: Patient Dispositional Trust (11 items): score range = 11–55, System Interaction Trust (11 items): score range = 11–55, Environmental Trust (5 items): score range = 5–25. Based on the distribution of scores in our validation sample, we propose the following preliminary interpretive guidelines: Low trust: total score ≤ 68; Moderate trust: total score between 69 and 96; High trust: total score ≥ 97. These ranges, derived from the 25th and 75th percentiles of our sample, should be validated in future studies. We recommend that healthcare institutions use these benchmarks to identify patients who may require additional support or human-led follow-up interventions.

### Future research directions and empirical extensions

While this study provides a validated tool, it also highlights several promising avenues for future research to further strengthen the evidence base and applicability of the scale. First, longitudinal validation is crucial to understand the dynamic nature of trust. Future studies should track patients’ trust scores over multiple time points to investigate how trust evolves with continued system use, exposure to system errors or successes, and changes in health status. This would provide insights into the temporal stability of the construct and identify critical windows for interventions to bolster trust. Second, cross-cultural adaptation and validation are essential for the global relevance of the scale. The current scale was developed within the specific cultural context of China. Future work should involve translating and adapting the scale for different cultural and healthcare settings to assess its measurement invariance and explore how cultural factors shape the antecedents of trust in artificial intelligence -based care. Finally, testing in diverse clinical populations is needed to establish generalizability. Future research should purposively recruit and validate the scale in populations such as elderly patients with limited digital literacy, individuals with chronic conditions requiring long-term follow-up, and patients from vulnerable socioeconomic backgrounds. This will ensure the scale is sensitive to the specific needs and concerns of different user groups.

### Study limitations

This study has several limitations. First, the use of a convenience sampling method—albeit structured by department (i.e., stratified convenience sampling)—carries an inherent risk of selection bias and may limit the generalizability of the findings. Since the participants were recruited from a single tertiary hospital in Jiangxi Province, their characteristics and experiences may not be representative of the broader patient population in China, particularly those in primary care settings or other regions. Moreover, the conceptualization and expression of trust are subject to cultural influences, and given that the scale was developed within a specific Chinese context, future studies should validate it in diverse cultural settings to assess its cross-cultural applicability. Future research should also employ probability sampling across multiple centers to further verify the scale’s psychometric properties. Finally, while the test–retest reliability assessed in a subgroup of 20 participants demonstrated good temporal stability (ICC = 0.901), the modest sample size for this analysis means the results should be interpreted with caution. They provide preliminary evidence of stability and require confirmation in larger cohorts. In addition, given the time constraints, this study examined only the reliability and validity of the scale without applying it in clinical settings. Future research will involve cross-sectional surveys and analysis of influencing factors in specific populations. The scale will also undergo further validation across different target groups to ensure its broad applicability and robustness.

## Conclusions

The final Artificial Intelligence-Based Follow-Up Trust Scale comprises 27 items across three dimensions: patient dispositional trust (11 items), system interaction trust (11 items), and environmental trust (5 items). This scale is positioned to be a valuable instrument that can support future healthcare policy development, inform targeted patient education, and guide the human-centered design of artificial intelligence systems. By providing a standardized metric, the scale can aid policymakers and hospital administrators in identifying systemic trust barriers and formulating strategies—such as establishing guidelines for algorithmic transparency and data security—that foster a trustworthy environment for artificial intelligence adoption. In clinical practice, the scale can help healthcare professionals identify patients with low trust levels, enabling tailored education and communication to alleviate concerns and improve engagement with artificial intelligence-facilitated care. Furthermore, the scale offers system developers actionable insights into the specific dimensions of trust that require optimization, such as interaction quality and reliability. This feedback is crucial for iteratively refining artificial intelligence systems to better align with patient needs and expectations, thereby enhancing user experience and adherence.
