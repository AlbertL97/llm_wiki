Title: The complexities of patient-centred conversational artificial intelligence

Summary:
Consumer-facing health chatbots powered by large language models (LLMs) are increasingly used for symptom assessment. However, chatbot development and evaluation often rely on cooperative, articulate, simulated patients. This study analyzed 2,053 real patient-chatbot conversations and found that communication patterns and expression of emotions vary widely across users. A patient simulator was developed that separately models clinical content, emotional state, conversational strategy, and communication style.

In a Turing-inspired evaluation of realism with 15 human graders, simulated conversations were nearly indistinguishable from real ones, with human graders achieving an accuracy of 55%.

The study used five distinct patient personae, across 1,164 clinician-graded cases, to evaluate the performance of four LLMs in urgency assessment. It was found that communication style can significantly alter triage outcomes.

Conclusion:
Patient-centred conversational artificial intelligence must accommodate communication diversity. Systems designed for idealised, rather than realistic, interactions risk underperforming and amplifying health disparities when deployed in the real world.
