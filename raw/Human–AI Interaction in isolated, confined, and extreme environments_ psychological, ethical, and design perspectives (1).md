## Abstract

Human activity is increasingly extending into environments marked by isolation, confinement, and extreme conditions, including long-duration space missions, polar research stations, intensive care units, and other high-risk settings. In these contexts, individuals must sustain performance and wellbeing under persistent cognitive, emotional, and social strain. Artificial intelligence (AI) systems are now deeply embedded in such environments, supporting decision making, monitoring, training, and, in some cases, psychological wellbeing. Yet research on AI in extreme settings has largely emphasized technical performance and automation, with comparatively limited attention to the lived experience of sustained Human–AI Interaction (HAI). This perspective paper argues that isolated, confined, or extreme (ICE) environments represent a uniquely revealing context for examining HAI. The psychological pressures characteristic of ICE settings—such as prolonged isolation, cognitive fatigue, stress, and high consequences of error—fundamentally shape how humans perceive, trust, and rely on AI systems. Drawing on interdisciplinary literature from human factors, psychology, and AI research, the paper conceptualizes ICE environments as a stress test for HAI, where issues of trust calibration, autonomy, transparency, and social attribution are amplified. Rather than treating AI solely as a decision aid, this perspective highlights how AI systems in ICE contexts may function as cognitive partners, social surrogates, or perceived teammates. The paper concludes by outlining key implications for the design, evaluation, and governance of AI systems intended for extreme environments, emphasizing the need for interaction-centered approaches that prioritize human experience alongside technical performance.

## 1 Introduction

Human activity is increasingly extending into environments characterized by isolation, confinement, and extreme conditions. From long-duration space missions and polar research stations to intensive care units, disaster zones, military deployments, and remote industrial operations, individuals and teams are required to perform complex tasks under sustained psychological, cognitive, and social strain (; ). These isolated, confined, or extreme (ICE) environments impose well-documented challenges, including sensory monotony, circadian disruption, social isolation, cognitive fatigue, heightened stress, and elevated consequences of error. As human presence in such contexts expands, so too does reliance on advanced technologies to sustain performance, safety, and wellbeing (;; ).

Artificial intelligence (AI) is rapidly becoming one of the most consequential technologies introduced into ICE environments (). AI systems now assist with decision support, environmental monitoring, anomaly detection, training, scheduling, communication, and, increasingly, psychological support (). Unlike many previous technological tools, AI systems do not merely extend physical or computational capacity; they interact with humans in adaptive, autonomous, and often socially mediated ways. As a result, the introduction of AI into ICE settings fundamentally reshapes how humans perceive, trust, and engage with technology, transforming tools into interactive partners within tightly coupled human–machine systems (;; ).

Despite rapid technological advances, much of the existing research on AI in extreme environments remains focused on performance optimization, automation, or engineering reliability. Comparatively less attention has been paid to the interactional dimension ()—that is, how humans experience, interpret, and adapt to sustained interaction with AI () under conditions of isolation, confinement, and stress. ICE environments amplify psychological processes such as reliance, trust formation, social attribution, and emotional attachment, making them uniquely revealing contexts for studying Human–AI Interaction (HAI). In such settings, AI may function not only as a decision aid, but also as a cognitive scaffold, a social surrogate, or even a perceived teammate (; ).

This perspective paper argues that ICE environments represent a critical frontier for understanding Human–AI Interaction. The psychological dynamics that emerge when humans interact with AI over prolonged periods, under constrained social contact and elevated risk, raise important questions about autonomy, dependency, trust calibration, ethical responsibility, and mental wellbeing. These questions extend beyond spaceflight or extreme missions (; ) and have growing relevance for more commonplace contexts such as remote work, telemedicine, and digitally mediated collaboration, where isolation and algorithmic support are increasingly normalized (;;; ).

This article is intentionally framed as a conceptual perspective and boundary-expansion argument rather than a formal theory-development or empirical modeling study. Its contribution does not lie in proposing a new standalone theoretical framework, but in positioning isolated, confined, and extreme (ICE) environments as an extreme boundary condition that amplifies latent dynamics within Human–AI Interaction. By conceptualizing ICE contexts as a stress-test lens, this paper highlights how psychological strain, prolonged isolation, communication constraints, and high-stakes consequences intensify trust calibration, automation bias, autonomy tensions, and social attribution processes. In doing so, it aims to sharpen theoretical sensitivity to context and to stimulate more precise empirical investigation of Human–AI Interaction under sustained stress conditions.

Rather than presenting new empirical findings, this paper offers a conceptual synthesis of existing research across psychology, human–computer interaction, human factors, and AI ethics, viewed through the lens of ICE environments. The aim is threefold. First, to articulate why ICE contexts uniquely shape Human–AI Interaction. Second, to examine the evolving roles AI systems play in supporting cognition, performance, and psychological health in these environments. Third, to identify key ethical, design, and research challenges that must be addressed as AI becomes more deeply embedded in human activity under extreme conditions. By doing so, this perspective seeks to contribute to a more human-centered, psychologically informed understanding of AI in ICE environments and to outline directions for future interdisciplinary research.

## 2 ICE environments as a unique context for Human–AI Interaction

Isolated, confined, or extreme (ICE) environments constitute a distinctive and demanding context for Human–AI Interaction (HAI). Unlike conventional workplaces or everyday human–computer settings, ICE environments impose sustained psychological, cognitive, and social pressures that fundamentally alter how humans perceive, rely on, and collaborate with intelligent systems. These conditions make ICE contexts particularly valuable for examining both the promises and vulnerabilities of AI-mediated support (;; ).

A defining feature of ICE environments is prolonged isolation and confinement. Whether in space missions, polar research stations, submarines, or remote medical facilities, individuals are separated from broader social networks for extended periods. This isolation reduces opportunities for social buffering, peer validation, and informal error correction, thereby increasing reliance on technological systems—not only for task execution but also for emotional regulation and decision reassurance.

ICE environments are also characterized by elevated cognitive fatigue and chronic stress. High workload demands, disrupted circadian rhythms, monotony, and persistent risk exposure place sustained strain on attention, memory, and executive functioning (). Under such conditions, human operators may become more susceptible to automation bias, over-reliance on AI recommendations, or, conversely, inappropriate distrust when system outputs conflict with fatigued human intuition (; ).

Another critical dimension is the scarcity of human social interaction. In settings where team sizes are small and social contact is limited, AI systems may take on roles that extend beyond instrumental decision support. Intelligent agents can become cognitive partners, social surrogates, or psychological anchors, blurring traditional boundaries between tool, teammate, and companion (;; ). This shift amplifies questions around trust calibration, anthropomorphism, and long-term human–AI co-adaptation.

ICE environments are further distinguished by the high consequences of errors. Mistakes in such contexts—whether in medical decision-making, mission control, or life-support management—can have irreversible outcomes. As a result, AI systems are often embedded in safety-critical workflows, where transparency, reliability, and interpretability are not merely desirable but essential. The tolerance for system failure is minimal, intensifying the ethical and operational stakes of HAI design (;;; ).

Finally, many ICE settings involve communication delays and limited external support, most notably in space exploration. When real-time guidance from Earth is unavailable, AI systems must operate with greater autonomy, and human operators must make decisions without immediate validation. This autonomy heightens the importance of mutual predictability and shared mental models between humans and AI agents (;; ).

Importantly, ICE environments are not homogeneous. Different contexts amplify distinct stressors and interactional dynamics. In long-duration space missions, communication delays and autonomy requirements dominate, reshaping reliance patterns and shared mental models (; ). In intensive care units, real-time human–AI collaboration under time pressure becomes central, intensifying coordination and accountability concerns (). In polar research stations or remote expeditions, prolonged social isolation and monotony may amplify social attribution and emotional reliance on AI systems (). Recognizing these contextual variations refines the stress-test framing and avoids treating ICE environments as a singular category.

Taken together, these characteristics position ICE environments as a natural stress test for Human–AI Interaction. They expose how AI systems perform under sustained uncertainty, psychological strain, and social deprivation—conditions that magnify both the strengths and weaknesses of current HAI paradigms. Insights gained from ICE contexts therefore have implications that extend well beyond extreme environments, informing the design of resilient, trustworthy, and human-centered AI systems for broader societal use. illustrates how isolated, confined, or extreme (ICE) environments function as a stress test for Human–AI Interaction.

## 3 Human–AI Interaction in extreme contexts

Human–AI Interaction (HAI) research has grown rapidly as AI systems become pervasive in daily life and specialized domains alike. A substantial body of work has examined trust, adaptation, and acceptance in AI-assisted environments, recognizing that AI's interactive capabilities fundamentally reshape how humans engage with technology (; ). Trust, in particular, emerges as a core construct in HAI research, reflecting users' willingness to rely on AI systems to perform tasks with anticipated outcomes (; ). Trust in AI is dynamic and context dependent, shaped by perceptions of transparency, reliability, and cognitive and emotional responses to AI behavior. In intelligent environments where AI agents are embedded across disparate subsystems, users must navigate multiple layers of trust, from basic task execution to system-level coordination (;; ). These findings provide a foundation for understanding how HAI processes might unfold in isolated, confined, or extreme (ICE) environments, where stress, risk, and prolonged interaction intensity are amplified relative to ordinary settings.

In ICE contexts, where humans may interact with AI over extended periods under pressure and limited social contact, the psychological mechanisms and outcomes of trust formation, reliance calibration, and adaptive engagement require more precise conceptualization. In particular, several constructs warrant clarification when examined under extreme conditions. Trust calibration refers to the dynamic alignment between human reliance and actual AI capability over time and context, especially in situations characterized by fatigue, uncertainty, and elevated consequences of error (). Automation bias in ICE settings denotes the heightened tendency to defer to AI recommendations when cognitive resources are depleted or when the perceived cost of independent verification is high (; ). Cognitive offloading describes the delegation of memory, monitoring, or decision functions to AI systems in order to conserve limited cognitive capacity under sustained strain (). Finally, social attribution under prolonged isolation refers to the process by which humans ascribe intentionality, agency, or social presence to AI systems when alternative sources of social interaction are restricted (). While these constructs are established in broader HAI literature, ICE environments may intensify their expression and interaction, making them particularly salient for theoretical and empirical investigation.

## 4 Psychological and behavioral dynamics of HAI in ICE environments

ICE environments impose sustained cognitive, emotional, and social challenges that uniquely shape human behavior. Long-duration spaceflight, polar expeditions, and extended missions in remote settings have been linked to monotony, sleep disruption, and stress, which affect attention, decision making, and interpersonal dynamics (;; ). When AI systems are introduced into these contexts, they do not merely assist with functional tasks; they become salient partners in people's cognitive and affective landscapes. Research in related HAI domains suggests that humans calibrate trust based on perceived competence, transparency, and predictability of AI behavior (; ). In high-stakes contexts, trust calibration must balance reliance with vigilance, avoiding both over-trust, which can lead to complacency, and under-trust, which undermines potential benefits. Dynamic HAI models, such as those predicting trust fluctuations over time, highlight the need to account for temporal and emotional trajectories of human engagement with AI (). Moreover, the psychological experience of interacting with AI—especially conversational or embodied agents—can evoke social and emotional responses that resemble human interaction patterns, a phenomenon intensively studied in human–computer interaction research (e.g., media naturalness and interaction perception models) and relevant to prolonged ICE contexts (; ).

## 5 Applied roles of AI in ICE settings

In ICE environments, AI systems are deployed across multiple functional roles beyond routine automation (). First, AI serves as a cognitive partner, offering decision support, pattern recognition, anomaly detection, and real-time monitoring that would exceed unaided human capacity. In high-risk environments such as ICU units or space missions, AI's predictive insights can help prioritize actions under uncertainty. Second, AI functions as a training and simulation companion, enabling virtual rehearsal of complex tasks (e.g., procedural rehearsal or emergency drills) that enhance preparedness. Third, AI is emerging as an emotional and psychological support agent, particularly through conversational systems or embodied interfaces that provide social presence in settings where human contact is limited. Empirical work exploring AI's potential in mental health and wellbeing outside ICE contexts suggests that AI interaction can influence subjective experience and coping mechanisms (;; ), which may translate into supportive functions in isolated and confined environments. These multiple roles, taken together, position AI not only as a tool for efficiency but also as an integral part of human experience in extreme conditions.

Across these roles, it becomes important to distinguish between interactive AI and autonomous AI within ICE settings. Interactive AI systems operate in continuous human-in-the-loop collaboration, adapting to user input and supporting joint decision processes, particularly in decision-support and training contexts (; ). Autonomous AI systems, by contrast, perform tasks or make decisions with limited real-time human oversight, especially under communication delays or operational constraints (; ). The balance between these modes carries significant implications for trust calibration, accountability structures, and the preservation of human agency in safety-critical environments.

Taken together, these roles position AI not merely as a tool for operational efficiency, but as an integral component of human cognitive, operational, and psychological functioning in extreme conditions.

## 6 Ethical, social, and design challenges

The integration of AI into human workflows and psychological spaces raises a constellation of ethical and design challenges amplified in ICE environments. A major concern is agency and autonomy: as AI systems assume more decision-relevant functions, determining when to defer to human judgment versus automated recommendations becomes ethically and operationally complex. Transparency and explainability are central to mitigating mistrust and facilitating appropriate reliance, yet many sophisticated AI systems remain opaque by design, complicating user comprehension and accountability (;; ). Research in human-centered AI underscores the importance of user agency and adaptive interaction paradigms, emphasizing interactive AI that responds to user states and promotes human control (;; ). Furthermore, the social impact of AI in environments where social bonds are already strained warrants attention: AI companionship might buffer isolation but also risk blurring relational boundaries, affecting group dynamics and individual wellbeing. Cross-cultural factors, ethical norms, and team composition in multinational ICE missions further complicate these issues, suggesting the imperative for design principles grounded in psychological theory and ethical foresight.

While AI may enhance performance and psychological support, it may also introduce new vulnerabilities. Overreliance on AI systems could gradually erode human resilience and adaptive expertise, particularly in prolonged missions. Increased explainability, though intended to foster transparency, may in high-pressure settings contribute to cognitive overload if explanations demand additional processing under fatigue. Furthermore, AI systems positioned as social surrogates risk emotional substitution effects, potentially altering group cohesion or reducing motivation for human-to-human interaction. These tensions underscore the need to treat AI integration in ICE environments as both enabling and potentially destabilizing.

## 7 Future research directions

Advancing HAI research in ICE contexts requires concerted interdisciplinary effort. First, longitudinal and ecological studies are needed to capture how human-AI relationships and trust evolve over extended exposure to extreme conditions. Dynamic measurement models that account for temporal shifts in trust, workload, and psychological states will be particularly valuable. Second, experimental paradigms that simulate ICE conditions with integrated AI agents can reveal behavioral and cognitive mechanisms that are difficult to observe in controlled laboratory settings. Third, theoretical frameworks that integrate human factors, behavioral psychology, and AI explainability principles are needed to guide the design of resilient, adaptive AI systems that support both performance and wellbeing. Finally, ethical frameworks tailored to ICE environments should be developed, addressing issues of autonomy, privacy, dependency, and cultural sensitivity. By situating HAI research within the broader psychological, social, and ethical landscape of ICE settings, these research directions can yield insights with both immediate operational relevance and broader implications for human–AI collaboration in diverse high-demand environments.

### 7.1 Illustrative research propositions

To guide future empirical inquiry, the following illustrative propositions emerge from the stress-test framing advanced in this perspective:

**Proposition 1**. Prolonged isolation and cognitive fatigue in ICE environments increase volatility in trust calibration toward AI systems over time.

**Proposition 2**. Communication latency moderates automation bias, such that greater delay strengthens reliance on autonomous AI decision support.

**Proposition 3**. Reduced human social interaction in ICE settings increases the likelihood of anthropomorphic social attribution toward interactive AI agents.

**Proposition 4**. In high-consequence environments, perceived risk amplifies delegation thresholds, leading users to shift more decision authority to AI under stress.

These propositions are intended as starting points for systematic empirical investigation rather than as elements of a fully specified theoretical model.

## 8 Conclusion

Isolated, confined, and extreme environments place humans at the edge of cognitive, emotional, and social endurance. As AI systems become increasingly integrated into these settings, they do more than automate tasks or process information; they actively shape how individuals think, feel, decide, and relate to their surroundings. This perspective has argued that Human–AI Interaction in ICE environments deserves focused conceptual and empirical attention, not only because of the operational demands of these contexts, but because they magnify fundamental questions about human–technology relationships.

ICE environments serve as natural laboratories for observing how trust in AI develops over time, how reliance and dependency emerge under stress, and how humans attribute agency and social meaning to non-human systems when human contact is limited. In these settings, AI may be perceived simultaneously as a tool, a teammate, a supervisor, or a companion. Such multifaceted roles challenge traditional distinctions between instrumental technology and social actors, highlighting the need for interaction-centered design principles that prioritize psychological safety, transparency, and human autonomy.

Importantly, the insights gained from ICE environments extend far beyond extreme missions. As societies increasingly adopt AI-mediated systems in healthcare, remote work, education, and crisis response, many of the psychological conditions that characterize ICE settings—reduced social contact, reliance on automated support, and high cognitive load—are becoming more widespread. Understanding how humans interact with AI in the most demanding environments can therefore inform the responsible design and deployment of AI in more routine contexts.

Looking forward, future research should move beyond isolated evaluations of AI performance and instead adopt longitudinal, interdisciplinary approaches that capture the evolving dynamics of Human–AI Interaction over time. Ethical considerations, including autonomy, accountability, cultural sensitivity, and mental health impacts, must be treated as central design concerns rather than secondary constraints. By integrating psychological theory, human-centered design, and technological innovation, researchers and practitioners can ensure that AI systems in ICE environments support not only efficiency and safety, but also human resilience and wellbeing.

In sum, Human–AI Interaction in isolated, confined, and extreme environments is not a peripheral concern, but a defining challenge of contemporary human–technology integration. Addressing it thoughtfully will be essential for sustaining human performance and psychological health in the most demanding contexts—and for shaping the future of human–AI collaboration more broadly.
