# Toward Personalized Social Robots for Child Well-being: Data Requirement Principles from a Recommender-System Perspective

## Summary

This work focuses on the crucial aspect of personalization for social robots deployed in clinical settings to support child well-being. The authors conceptualize personalization as a recommendation problem, drawing parallels with recommender-system frameworks that involve user profiling, ranking, and responsible computing. However, they highlight significant data challenges that hinder effective instantiation: children's states are dynamic and shift rapidly, making static user descriptions unreliable. Furthermore, signals indicating the effectiveness of robot actions are weak and indirect, and data collection is often hampered by infrequent child visits, anonymization protocols that break identity linkage across sessions, and observational biases due to the inability to randomize care.

To address these data bottlenecks, the paper proposes four key data principles:

*   **Integrated Profile:** A comprehensive profile that captures a child's evolving state and characteristics.
*   **Effectiveness Signals:** Methods to capture direct or strong indirect evidence of how robot actions impact a child's well-being.
*   **Linkable Coverage:** Strategies to ensure data from different visits or interactions can be linked to form a coherent user history.
*   **Exposure Record:** Logging of interactions and robot actions at the time of data collection to provide context for future analysis.

The authors identify the specific requirements each of these principles fulfills and frame them as actionable guidelines for future data collection efforts in the domain of social robots for child well-being.
