## Main content

This paper conceptualizes isolated, confined, and extreme (ICE) environments as a unique boundary condition that amplifies the cognitive, social, and operational demands of human-AI collaboration (source: Human–AI Interaction in isolated, confined, and extreme environments_ psychological, ethical, and design perspectives.md).

### 1. ICE Environments as a Stress-Test Lens
ICE environments—ranging from long-duration space missions and polar stations to submarines and intensive care units (ICUs)—impose chronic cognitive fatigue, circadian disruption, sensory monotony, and high consequences of error (source: Human–AI Interaction in isolated, confined, and extreme environments_ psychological, ethical, and design perspectives.md). Rather than viewing AI strictly as an optimization tool, this perspective highlights how AI acts as an active cognitive partner, social surrogate, or perceived teammate in high-stress, safety-critical workflows (source: Human–AI Interaction in isolated, confined, and extreme environments_ psychological, ethical, and design perspectives.md).

### 2. Evolving Roles and Modalities of AI
In extreme environments, AI systems are deployed across distinct collaborative paradigms (source: Human–AI Interaction in isolated, confined, and extreme environments_ psychological, ethical, and design perspectives.md):
- **Interactive AI**: Involves continuous human-in-the-loop collaboration where the AI adapts to user inputs (e.g., real-time clinical decision support or interactive mission training) (source: Human–AI Interaction in isolated, confined, and extreme environments_ psychological, ethical, and design perspectives.md).
- **Autonomous AI**: Operates with minimal real-time human oversight. This is particularly crucial in deep-space exploration where communication latency restricts real-time Earth support, requiring high mutual predictability and shared mental models between the operator and the system (source: Human–AI Interaction in isolated, confined, and extreme environments_ psychological, ethical, and design perspectives.md).
- **Social Surrogates / Companions**: Conversational or embodied agents that provide social presence and emotional anchors to buffer the effects of prolonged social isolation and sensory monotony (source: Human–AI Interaction in isolated, confined, and extreme environments_ psychological, ethical, and design perspectives.md).

### 3. Key Psychological and Interactional Constructs
The paper details four main psychological phenomena that become intensified under ICE conditions (source: Human–AI Interaction in isolated, confined, and extreme environments_ psychological, ethical, and design perspectives.md):
- **Trust Calibration**: The dynamic alignment between human reliance and actual AI capabilities. Under stress, trust calibration becomes highly volatile, fluctuating between excessive compliance and rapid rejection (source: Human–AI Interaction in isolated, confined, and extreme environments_ psychological, ethical, and design perspectives.md).
- **Automation Bias**: The uncritical deference to automated recommendations when cognitive resources are depleted or the effort required to verify recommendations is high (source: Human–AI Interaction in isolated, confined, and extreme environments_ psychological, ethical, and design perspectives.md).
- **Cognitive Offloading**: Delegating monitoring, memory, or decision functions to the AI to conserve limited cognitive capacity under sustained strain, which risks causing user deskilling and a loss of adaptive expertise (source: Human–AI Interaction in isolated, confined, and extreme environments_ psychological, ethical, and design perspectives.md).
- **Social Attribution**: Ascribing intentionality, agency, or social presence to AI systems when alternative human social interaction is restricted (source: Human–AI Interaction in isolated, confined, and extreme environments_ psychological, ethical, and design perspectives.md).

### 4. Illustrative Research Propositions
To guide future empirical inquiry, the authors propose four conceptual research propositions (source: Human–AI Interaction in isolated, confined, and extreme environments_ psychological, ethical, and design perspectives.md):
- **Proposition 1**: Prolonged isolation and cognitive fatigue in ICE environments increase volatility in trust calibration toward AI systems over time (source: Human–AI Interaction in isolated, confined, and extreme environments_ psychological, ethical, and design perspectives.md).
- **Proposition 2**: Communication latency moderates automation bias, such that greater delay strengthens reliance on autonomous AI decision support (source: Human–AI Interaction in isolated, confined, and extreme environments_ psychological, ethical, and design perspectives.md).
- **Proposition 3**: Reduced human social interaction in ICE settings increases the likelihood of anthropomorphic social attribution toward interactive AI agents (source: Human–AI Interaction in isolated, confined, and extreme environments_ psychological, ethical, and design perspectives.md).
- **Proposition 4**: In high-consequence environments, perceived risk amplifies delegation thresholds, leading users to shift more decision authority to AI under stress (source: Human–AI Interaction in isolated, confined, and extreme environments_ psychological, ethical, and design perspectives.md).
