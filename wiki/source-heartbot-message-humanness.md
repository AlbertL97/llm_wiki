## Main content

This study presents a secondary data analysis of the HeartBot trial, evaluating how users perceive a health chatbot's identity and exploring the role of message characteristics in this perception (source: Message Humanness as a Predictor of AI’s Perception as Human_ Secondary Data Analysis of the HeartBot Study.md).

### 1. Study Design and Methodology
- **Objective**: Identify interaction and user characteristics associated with perceiving the chatbot as a human versus an artificial agent (source: Message Humanness as a Predictor of AI’s Perception as Human_ Secondary Data Analysis of the HeartBot Study.md).
- **Participants & Intervention**: 92 women (mean age 45.9) interacted once with "HeartBot," a fully automated SMS text-based chatbot built on Google Dialogflow CX and Twilio to increase heart attack awareness (source: Message Humanness as a Predictor of AI’s Perception as Human_ Secondary Data Analysis of the HeartBot Study.md).
- **Identity Disclosure**: The chatbot introduced itself as "HeartBot" but did not explicitly specify that it was an automated AI system (source: Message Humanness as a Predictor of AI’s Perception as Human_ Secondary Data Analysis of the HeartBot Study.md).
- **Measures**: Collected age, race, education, previous chatbot use, conversation length (words and minutes), message humanness (5-item anthropomorphism scale: natural/fake, human-like/machine-like, conscious/unconscious, lifelike/artificial, adaptive/rigid; Cronbach's $\alpha = 0.90$), message effectiveness (5-item scale: effective, helpful, beneficial, adequate, supportive; Cronbach's $\alpha = 0.93$), and general attitude toward AI (source: Message Humanness as a Predictor of AI’s Perception as Human_ Secondary Data Analysis of the HeartBot Study.md).

### 2. Key Findings and Predictors of Perceived Identity
- **Widespread Misperception**: While two-thirds ($66\%$) of the participants correctly identified the chatbot as an artificial agent, one-third ($34\%$) incorrectly believed they were texting a human professional, despite the chatbot's explicit name (source: Message Humanness as a Predictor of AI’s Perception as Human_ Secondary Data Analysis of the HeartBot Study.md).
- **Message Humanness as the Sole Predictor**: In multivariable logistic regression, only the perceived **message humanness** score was significantly associated with perceiving the chatbot as human (AOR 2.37, 95% CI 1.26-4.48; $P = .007$), controlling for demographics, past chatbot use, and attitudes (source: Message Humanness as a Predictor of AI’s Perception as Human_ Secondary Data Analysis of the HeartBot Study.md).
- **Non-significant Factors**: Message effectiveness ($P = .28$), conversation length ($P = .93$), general attitudes toward AI ($P = .99$), and sociodemographic background (age, race, education) did not significantly influence identity perception (source: Message Humanness as a Predictor of AI’s Perception as Human_ Secondary Data Analysis of the HeartBot Study.md). Perceiving the chatbot as human is determined by the linguistic encoding and decoding of conversational naturalness, not the utility or impact of the dialogue.

### 3. Theoretical and Practical Implications
- **Expectancy Violation Effect**: A chatbot's perceived identity shapes user expectations. If a user perceives the chatbot as a human, they evaluate its performance based on human standards, leading to poorer evaluations if it fails to meet those standards (source: Message Humanness as a Predictor of AI’s Perception as Human_ Secondary Data Analysis of the HeartBot Study.md). Conversely, if they perceive it as an artificial agent, they judge its performance based on chatbot stereotypes, which are more easily satisfied.
- **Conversational Cues**: Conversational cues such as polite statements, empathetic phrases, and acknowledging previous responses (message contingency) enhance the social presence and perceived humanness of the chatbot (source: Message Humanness as a Predictor of AI’s Perception as Human_ Secondary Data Analysis of the HeartBot Study.md).
- **Ethical Prescriptions**: Given the high rate of misperception ($34\%$), chatbot developers must explicitly disclose the automated AI identity of the system at the beginning of interactions to prevent unethical deception and ensure users maintain calibrated expectations, especially in sensitive domains like healthcare (source: Message Humanness as a Predictor of AI’s Perception as Human_ Secondary Data Analysis of the HeartBot Study.md).
