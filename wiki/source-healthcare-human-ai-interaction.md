## Main content

This source addresses the critical gap between laboratory evaluation of medical AI systems and their real-world clinical performance, emphasizing that the value of healthcare AI is ultimately realized in the quality of its interaction with human professionals (source: Examining human-AI interaction in real-world healthcare beyond the laboratory.md).

### 1. The Real-World Clinical Evaluation Gap
Most medical AI research concentrates on laboratory performance metrics (e.g., sensitivity, specificity, AUROC). However, these studies fail to capture the complexities of clinical settings, which involve:
- **Workload & Environmental Pressures**: Time constraints, cognitive distractions, and ergonomic limitations (source: Examining human-AI interaction in real-world healthcare beyond the laboratory.md).
- **Institutional Variabilities**: Disease variability, clinical workflow steps, and institutional standards.
- **Unintended Consequences**: A system that demonstrates high accuracy in the laboratory can cause clinician frustration and workflow disruption when deployed in daily practice (source: Examining human-AI interaction in real-world healthcare beyond the laboratory.md).

### 2. Evaluative Dimensions: Task Performance vs. User Experience (UX)
The authors emphasize that a complete evaluation requires measuring two distinct, interlinked dimensions (source: Examining human-AI interaction in real-world healthcare beyond the laboratory.md):
- **Task Performance**: Objective measures of efficiency, accuracy, and error rates (e.g., task completion time, patient throughput, and clinical safety events avoided) (source: Examining human-AI interaction in real-world healthcare beyond the laboratory.md).
- **User Experience (UX)**: A holistic view (defined by ISO 9241-210) that goes beyond usability. It encompasses task-related (pragmatic usability) and non-task-related (hedonic pleasure, stimulation, and professional identity formation) qualities, alongside clinician emotions (source: Examining human-AI interaction in real-world healthcare beyond the laboratory.md). Poor UX Usability directly correlates with clinician burnout and the adoption of dangerous workflow workarounds.

### 3. The Proposed Multimodal Toolkit
To scale real-world evaluation, the authors propose a practical, scalable toolkit combining real-time and non-real-time instruments (source: Examining human-AI interaction in real-world healthcare beyond the laboratory.md):
- **Real-Time Assessment**:
  - *Consumer Wearable Sensors*: Smartwatches (rather than diagnostic-grade electrodes) to scale physiological metrics comfortably, tracking heart rate, heart rate variability (HRV), blood pressure, respiration, and electrodermal activity (source: Examining human-AI interaction in real-world healthcare beyond the laboratory.md).
  - *Embedded Ecological Momentary Assessment (EMA)*: Brief, validated self-report items integrated directly within Clinical Information Systems (CIS) or companion app-enabled devices to capture stress, cognitive effort, alertness, and system trust with minimal workflow interruption (source: Examining human-AI interaction in real-world healthcare beyond the laboratory.md).
  - *Quantitative Video Observations*: Cameras coupled with Video-Language Models (VLMs) and computer vision to automatically detect facial expressions, body posture, and interaction patterns (source: Examining human-AI interaction in real-world healthcare beyond the laboratory.md).
- **Non-Real-Time Assessment**:
  - *Baseline Physiology*: Standardizing baseline heart rate and autonomic activity.
  - *Contextual Questionnaires*: Assessing individual variables (demographics, workplace experience) and digitalization characteristics (digital competence, AI acceptance, and adherence guidelines) (source: Examining human-AI interaction in real-world healthcare beyond the laboratory.md).

### 4. Regulation & Trust Failures in Clinical Workflows
- **Cognitive Interfaces**: Medical AI systems are not merely software tools; they act as interfaces that alter the clinician's cognitive and emotional state. Poorly engineered systems risk causing **automation bias** (uncritical acceptance of AI output), **complacency bias**, and clinician **deskilling** (source: Examining human-AI interaction in real-world healthcare beyond the laboratory.md).
- **Thai Retinopathy Case Study**: An observational study by Google Health on a deep learning tool for detecting diabetic retinopathy in Thailand clinics revealed that model accuracy did not guarantee workflow success. High image-rejection rates (rebutted by human screeners) increased staff workload, escalated stress, and delayed patient wait times, highlighting the critical link between user experience, trust calibration, and system utility (source: Examining human-AI interaction in real-world healthcare beyond the laboratory.md).
