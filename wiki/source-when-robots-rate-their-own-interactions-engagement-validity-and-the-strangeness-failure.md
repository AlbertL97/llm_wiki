# When Robots Rate Their Own Interactions: Engagement Validity and the Strangeness Failure

## Summary
This paper introduces the concept of "inverted evaluation" in Human-Robot Interaction (HRI), where Large Language Models (LLMs) are used to complete standardized interaction evaluation questionnaires from the robot's own perspective. The study found that while LLMs could achieve moderate to strong agreement with human ratings on dimensions like satisfaction and enjoyment, they consistently failed to accurately assess the 'comfort/strangeness' dimension, often inverting it. This suggests that current LLMs may lack the necessary introspective or affective capabilities to provide a true robot perspective on interaction quality, and that supplementary data modalities are required for more comprehensive AI self-assessment in HRI.

## Key Findings

*   **Inverted Evaluation Framework:** Proposes using LLMs to complete HRI evaluation instruments from the robot's viewpoint.
*   **Engagement Dimension Agreement:** LLMs demonstrated moderate to strong agreement with human evaluations on dimensions such as satisfaction and enjoyment in interaction studies.
*   **Strangeness Failure:** A systematic inversion of the comfort/strangeness dimension was observed across multiple LLM models and in embodied robot trials, indicating a disconnect between perceived engagement and affective comfort from the AI's perspective.
*   **Limitations of LLMs:** Current LLMs are identified as lacking access to internal affective states necessary for accurate self-assessment of nuanced interaction constructs like strangeness.
*   **Need for Multimodal Data:** The study argues for the incorporation of supplementary data (e.g., physiological signals, gaze, proxemics) to overcome the limitations of solely relying on behavioral proxies and LLM self-reports for HRI evaluation.

## Methodology

*   **Study 1:** Five LLMs evaluated 25 interactions using HRI-CUES, Godspeed, and RoSAS questionnaires. This involved a large dataset of N = 1,522 evaluations.
*   **Study 2:** A Nao robot powered by Claude Sonnet 4.5 replicated the findings in live interactions with human participants (N = 4), including real-time turn-by-turn assessments.

## Implications

*   Establishes boundary conditions for utilizing LLMs as evaluators in HRI.
*   Highlights the critical need for deeper understanding and measurement of robot 'affective states' or their proxies.
*   Suggests future directions for HRI evaluation that combine AI self-reporting with objective or multimodal human-centric data.
