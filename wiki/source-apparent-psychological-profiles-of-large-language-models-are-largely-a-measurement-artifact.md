# Apparent Psychological Profiles of Large Language Models are Largely a Measurement Artifact

This paper investigates the validity of applying psychological instruments developed for humans to assess the psychological profiles of Large Language Models (LLMs).

## Key Findings:

*   **Measurement Artifact:** The study demonstrates that the observed psychological profiles of LLMs are largely a result of how the measurement instruments function, rather than inherent, stable psychological traits of the models.
*   **Directional Response Bias:** Differences in responses between LLMs are predominantly driven by a directional response bias – a tendency to favor one end of a scale or one option regardless of item content. This bias accounts for a significantly higher proportion of variation in LLMs (81-90%) compared to humans (9-16%).
*   **Model Capability and Bias:** While higher model capability can reduce this bias, it does not eliminate it.
*   **Reliability Prediction:** The apparent reliability of an instrument is strongly predicted by its response orthogonality (the degree to which trait and bias directions oppose each other). Instruments with low orthogonality yield higher apparent reliability for LLMs because the bias masks the true trait.
*   **Manufactured Profiles:** The perceived profile of an LLM can be manipulated through selective item choices within an instrument.
*   **Call for New Assessments:** The authors call for the development of dedicated assessment tools for LLMs that prioritize response orthogonality, as instruments borrowed from human psychology may lack validity for AI.
