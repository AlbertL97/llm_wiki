## Main content

This literature survey reviews experimental human-robot interaction (HRI) studies published in 2018 and 2019 to evaluate how trust is defined, categorized, and measured in human-machine environments (source: untitled.md).

### 1. Two Main Categories of HRI Trust
The authors propose dividing HRI trust into two distinct dimensions based on the nature of the interaction (source: untitled.md):
- **Performance-Based Trust**: A user's trust in the robot's capability to successfully execute physical tasks, manage goals, and behave reliably without constant human supervision (source: untitled.md).
- **Relation-Based Trust**: A user's trust in the robot as a social partner, expecting it to behave appropriately, adhere to social norms, and operate as part of a social environment rather than merely acting as an industrial tool (source: untitled.md).

### 2. Measurement Methods and Conflation
The survey identifies several methodological patterns and challenges in HRI trust research (source: untitled.md):
- **Objective Performance Measures**: Performance-based trust is frequently evaluated using objective metrics. A primary indicator is **intervention or takeover frequency** (the rate at which the human operator steps in to override the robot's decisions or take over the task) (source: untitled.md).
- **Subjective Relationship Measures**: While interest in relation-based social trust has grown, there is a critical **lack of standardized, objective measurement methods** (source: untitled.md). Studies rely almost exclusively on subjective self-report questionnaires (e.g., Godspeed or MDMT).
- **Conceptual Conflation**: Existing research frequently conflates performance-based trust (robot-as-tool capability) and relation-based trust (robot-as-social-agent norm adherence), making it difficult to isolate which factors drive user trust dynamics (source: untitled.md).

### 3. Recommendations
The authors recommend that future HRI research (source: untitled.md):
- Explicitly define the type of trust being investigated to avoid conceptual conflation.
- Develop objective, standardized paradigms to measure relation-based social trust beyond subjective questionnaires.
