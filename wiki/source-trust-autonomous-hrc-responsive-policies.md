## Main content

### Overview
This pilot study ($N=29$ participants) investigates how a social robot's autonomous interaction policy influences human trust during in-person collaboration, operating without Wizard-of-Oz control (source: Trust in Autonomous Human-Robot Collaboration_ Effects of Responsive Interaction Policies.md).

### Experimental Conditions
Participants completed collaborative tasks with a mobile robot under two interaction policies:
1. **Responsive Policy**: The robot proactively adapted its dialogue and assistance based on real-time affect inference and workspace states.
2. **Reactive Policy**: The robot only responded to direct user prompts with task-relevant information.

### Crucial Findings
1. **Responsive Policy Advantage**: Under viable communication conditions, the responsive policy led to **significantly higher post-interaction trust**, despite no differences in overall task performance.
2. **Vulnerability to Communication Breakdown**:
   - As language-mediated communication degraded (speech recognition errors), the trust advantage of the responsive policy was completely lost.
   - **Affective and experiential trust components** (feeling comfortable, liking the robot) were far more sensitive to communication failures than cognitive judgments of utility/reliability.
   - Under communication breakdown, users' trust ratings became erratic, indicating a collapse in calibration (source: Trust in Autonomous Human-Robot Collaboration_ Effects of Responsive Interaction Policies.md).
