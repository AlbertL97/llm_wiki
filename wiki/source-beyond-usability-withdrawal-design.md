# Source Summary: Beyond Usability: Withdrawal Design in Social Robotics

**Summary**: An 8-week randomized controlled trial (N=40) demonstrating that while continuous social robot access reduces anxiety, it decreases children's social motivation and emotion recognition gains compared to withdrawing the robot, suggesting "withdrawal design" is crucial to avoid siloing relationships.

**Sources**:
- Beyond Usability - Withdrawal Design in Social Robotics.md

**Last updated**: 2026-06-16

---

## Key Findings

- **The Trial**: Conducted an 8-week home-based randomized controlled trial (N=40) evaluating children with autism interacting with a consumer social robot (Qrobot).
- **Usability vs. Outcomes**: The robot achieved exceptionally high System Usability Scale (SUS) scores. However, high usability did not correlate with positive ecological outcomes.
- **Diverging Effects**:
  - *Anxiety Reduction*: Continued access to the robot reduced anxiety as measured by standard scales (SCARED/RCADS).
  - *Social Deficits*: Continued access resulted in lower parent-reported social motivation (SMS) and weaker gains in emotion recognition (RMET) compared to children from whom the robot was withdrawn.
- **The Scaffold Paradox**: Social behavior became siloed within the child-robot dyad. When the robot was removed, it acted as a scaffold that prompted children to seek out human-to-human interaction.
- **UXR Design Shift**: Engagement and retention metrics can mask ecological downsides. Success in social robotics should be judged by designed separation ("withdrawal design") that bridges back to human relationships.
