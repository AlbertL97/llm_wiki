# Correct Yourself, Keep My Trust: How Self-Correction and Social Connection Shape Credibility in Social Chatbots

This paper investigates the psychological dynamics of user trust and credibility when social chatbots make and correct mistakes. It highlights the importance of how chatbots recover from errors, especially given their increasing integration into everyday life and their propensity for generating convincing but inaccurate information.

## Key Findings
*   **Self-Correction Preserves Credibility:** While all tested correction strategies (webpage retraction, self-correction, expert chatbot correction) effectively corrected factual errors, only self-correction by the original chatbot maintained its credibility. Participants rated self-correcting chatbots significantly higher in both trustworthiness and perceived expertise.
*   **Social Connection Amplifies Self-Correction:** The strength of a user's social connection with the chatbot (assessed via social attraction and self-disclosure) significantly enhanced the impact of error correction on belief change, but only when the chatbot self-corrected. External corrections entirely nullified this beneficial link.
*   **External Correction Damages Credibility:** Outsourcing corrections to external sources (a webpage or an expert chatbot) significantly damaged the original chatbot's trustworthiness and perceived expertise, despite correcting the factual error.

## Implications
*   **Design for Self-Correction:** Chatbot developers should prioritize enabling chatbots to identify and correct their own errors directly within the interaction.
*   **Invest in Social Connection:** Building strong social connections with users is not just a design aesthetic but a functional mechanism that enhances the effectiveness of error correction and helps maintain long-term credibility.
