# Source: A Comparison of Avatar-, Video-, and Robot-Mediated Interaction on Users’ Trust in Expertise

**Summary**: A summary page for the research article comparing trust development and advice-seeking behaviors across video-, avatar-, and robot-mediated communication channels, exploring how media representation affects a user's ability to identify expertise.

## Main content

This research investigates the influence of communication media representations—specifically video projection, virtual avatars, and physical humanoid robots—on trust formation in user–adviser relationships (source: A Comparison of Avatar-, Video-, and Robot-Mediated Interaction on Users’ Trust in Expertise.md).

### 1. Research Objectives and Methodology
The authors compared how users evaluate expertise and calibrate trust across three distinct media formats using a general-knowledge quiz paradigm (source: A Comparison of Avatar-, Video-, and Robot-Mediated Interaction on Users’ Trust in Expertise.md):
- **Participants & Setup**: 24 participants completed a 30-question quiz (plus a final high-stakes question) where the difficulty of the questions necessitated seeking advice from one of two advisors (source: A Comparison of Avatar-, Video-, and Robot-Mediated Interaction on Users’ Trust in Expertise.md).
- **Expertise Manipulation**: The advisors were recorded from the same female actor at different levels of training: an **expert advisor** (providing 93% correct answers with confident non-verbal cues) and a **non-expert advisor** (providing 36% correct answers with lower-confidence cues like reduced eye contact) (source: A Comparison of Avatar-, Video-, and Robot-Mediated Interaction on Users’ Trust in Expertise.md).
- **Media Pairings**: Six between-subject conditions paired two of the three media representations (Robot vs. Avatar, Robot vs. Video, Avatar vs. Video) with counterbalanced expert/non-expert designations (source: A Comparison of Avatar-, Video-, and Robot-Mediated Interaction on Users’ Trust in Expertise.md).
- **Measures**: Trust was assessed using the behavioral **advice-seeking rate** (proportion of questions for which an advisor's help was sought) and a post-experimental **subjective trust questionnaire** (source: A Comparison of Avatar-, Video-, and Robot-Mediated Interaction on Users’ Trust in Expertise.md).

### 2. Key Findings and Media Biases
While participants generally learned to distinguish the expert from the non-expert over time, the choice of communication medium introduced significant biases (source: A Comparison of Avatar-, Video-, and Robot-Mediated Interaction on Users’ Trust in Expertise.md):
- **The Avatar Negative Trust Bias**: Avatars generated a distinct negative bias. In both Robot vs. Avatar and Avatar vs. Video conditions, participants were significantly less likely to seek advice from the avatar, regardless of whether it was the expert (source: A Comparison of Avatar-, Video-, and Robot-Mediated Interaction on Users’ Trust in Expertise.md). Self-reports confirmed this, with participants rating the avatar lowest in subjective trust and noting that it "didn’t seem real" (source: A Comparison of Avatar-, Video-, and Robot-Mediated Interaction on Users’ Trust in Expertise.md).
- **Robot Embodiment Compensation**: Physical presence (embodiment) of the humanoid robot ( Engineered Arts Robothespian™) was found to positively influence trust, offsetting its lack of a realistic personal appearance (source: A Comparison of Avatar-, Video-, and Robot-Mediated Interaction on Users’ Trust in Expertise.md). In Robot vs. Avatar conditions, the physical robot was strongly preferred.
- **Robot vs. Video Equivalence**: In the Robot vs. Video condition, there was no statistically significant difference in advice-seeking rates between the expert robot and the expert video (source: A Comparison of Avatar-, Video-, and Robot-Mediated Interaction on Users’ Trust in Expertise.md). The trust-enhancing benefit of the robot's physical presence offset the trust benefits of the video's realistic appearance and detailed identity cues (source: A Comparison of Avatar-, Video-, and Robot-Mediated Interaction on Users’ Trust in Expertise.md).
- **Video Trust Overestimation**: Video representations of the actor standing in front of a natural background were highly trusted due to the presence of real appearance cues (source: A Comparison of Avatar-, Video-, and Robot-Mediated Interaction on Users’ Trust in Expertise.md). In the Avatar vs. Video condition, when the non-expert was video-mediated, participants' preference for video led them to disregard the expert avatar's advice (source: A Comparison of Avatar-, Video-, and Robot-Mediated Interaction on Users’ Trust in Expertise.md).
- **High-Stakes Verification Illusion**: For the final high-stakes question, participants in the Avatar vs. Video (avatar is expert) condition shifted their preference back to the video advisor (source: A Comparison of Avatar-, Video-, and Robot-Mediated Interaction on Users’ Trust in Expertise.md). Subjective reports revealed an illusion of accuracy: users believed they could look at the video's detailed facial expressions to read whether she was certain, leading them to overestimate their ability to detect trustworthiness in video-mediated channels (source: A Comparison of Avatar-, Video-, and Robot-Mediated Interaction on Users’ Trust in Expertise.md).

### 3. Practical Implications for Design
System developers often favor video- or avatar-mediated setups to reduce cost and maintenance. However, this study suggests that designers must account for how these media choices distort trust (source: A Comparison of Avatar-, Video-, and Robot-Mediated Interaction on Users’ Trust in Expertise.md):
- Avatars may impair a user's ability to correctly calibrate trust, potentially causing them to reject expert guidance due to a lack of perceived realism (source: A Comparison of Avatar-, Video-, and Robot-Mediated Interaction on Users’ Trust in Expertise.md).
- Physical robot embodiments can be utilized in collaborative or advice-seeking settings to build trust and social telepresence, compensating for anonymous or synthetic visual appearances (source: A Comparison of Avatar-, Video-, and Robot-Mediated Interaction on Users’ Trust in Expertise.md).
