## Main content

This article unpacks the psychology of human-AI interaction, arguing that reframing "trust" as "reliance" provides a more accurate and actionable model for user experience design (source: The Psychology of Trust in AI_ Why “Relying on AI” Matters More than “Trusting It”.md).

### 1. The Neurocognitive Distinction Between Trust and Reliance
The author outlines a key psychological and neurological divide between how we interact with humans versus machines (source: The Psychology of Trust in AI_ Why “Relying on AI” Matters More than “Trusting It”.md):
- **Human Trust**: Built on social and evolutionary signals like empathy, shared intentions, reputation, and loyalty. Neuroimaging studies show that human trust is processed in dedicated brain networks involving the **thalamic-striatal regions and the frontal cortex** (source: The Psychology of Trust in AI_ Why “Relying on AI” Matters More than “Trusting It”.md).
- **AI Reliance**: Driven by separate psychological processes. A user's propensity to trust other humans does not predict their willingness to rely on AI systems (such as voice assistants, chatbots, or autonomous vehicles) (source: The Psychology of Trust in AI_ Why “Relying on AI” Matters More than “Trusting It”.md). AI lacks emotions and social intentions, so the brain processes interaction with it as reliance on a tool rather than a social bond.

### 2. Why "Rely" is Better Than "Trust"
"Trust" implies an emotional expectation of benevolence or loyalty, which does not fit algorithms. In contrast, "rely" shifts the design focus onto usability and performance criteria (source: The Psychology of Trust in AI_ Why “Relying on AI” Matters More than “Trusting It”.md):
- **Consistency**: The predictability of the AI's behavior across different contexts (source: The Psychology of Trust in AI_ Why “Relying on AI” Matters More than “Trusting It”.md).
- **Transparency**: The ease with which a user can understand the rationale behind an AI recommendation (source: The Psychology of Trust in AI_ Why “Relying on AI” Matters More than “Trusting It”.md).
- **Controllability**: The user's ability to intervene, adjust, or override the system when necessary (source: The Psychology of Trust in AI_ Why “Relying on AI” Matters More than “Trusting It”.md).
- **Feedback Loops**: The system's capacity to learn from human corrections and adapt over time (source: The Psychology of Trust in AI_ Why “Relying on AI” Matters More than “Trusting It”.md).

### 3. Five Building Blocks of User Reliance
To foster stable user reliance, UX designers must implement five core building blocks (source: The Psychology of Trust in AI_ Why “Relying on AI” Matters More than “Trusting It”.md):
1. **Predictability**: Fulfilling expectations and communicating clear boundaries of what the system can and cannot do to mitigate human aversion to uncertainty (source: The Psychology of Trust in AI_ Why “Relying on AI” Matters More than “Trusting It”.md).
2. **Explainability**: Providing clear, user-centered rationales for recommendations rather than complex technical documentation (source: The Psychology of Trust in AI_ Why “Relying on AI” Matters More than “Trusting It”.md).
3. **Error Management**: Transparently admitting system errors and communicating uncertainty. Expressing confidence (e.g., "I'm 70% confident in this answer") gives the user the agency to decide whether to check the output (source: The Psychology of Trust in AI_ Why “Relying on AI” Matters More than “Trusting It”.md).
4. **Controllability and Agency**: Giving the user the ultimate authority to pause, override, or correct the AI (source: The Psychology of Trust in AI_ Why “Relying on AI” Matters More than “Trusting It”.md).
5. **Consistency with Values**: Aligning the AI's functions and safeguards with established ethical and social norms (source: The Psychology of Trust in AI_ Why “Relying on AI” Matters More than “Trusting It”.md).
