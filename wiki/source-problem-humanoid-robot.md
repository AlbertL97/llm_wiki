## Main content

This source provides a critical analysis of the current state of consumer humanoid robotics, focusing on 1X Technologies' NEO robot (source: The Problem with this Humanoid Robot.md).

### 1. NEO Humanoid Robot Specifications
- **Physical Design**: NEO is a bipedal robot designed for home use, standing 5'6" tall, weighing 66 pounds, and featuring a 4-hour battery life with self-charging capabilities (source: The Problem with this Humanoid Robot.md). It is human-shaped, featuring two legs and ten fingers to match human dexterity in domestic spaces.
- **Commercial Model**: Offered via pre-order for a $500/month subscription or a flat $20,000 purchase price, requiring a $200 refundable deposit (source: The Problem with this Humanoid Robot.md).

### 2. The Autonomy Gap and Teleoperation
- **Demonstration Reality**: While promotional materials imply autonomous household operation, a WSJ hands-on demonstration by Joanna Stern revealed that 100% of the tasks shown—including folding laundry and loading a dishwasher—were teleoperated by a human operator in another room using a VR headset and controllers (source: The Problem with this Humanoid Robot.md).
- **Limited Autonomy**: In a 10-minute promotional keynote, only two short scenes were explicitly labeled as autonomous: walking to open a door ("get the door") and carrying an empty cup ("put away the dishes") (source: The Problem with this Humanoid Robot.md).

### 3. "Expert Mode" and Privacy Implications
- **Teleoperation-as-a-Service**: To handle tasks beyond its autonomous capabilities, NEO features "Expert Mode," which allows users to schedule a remote 1X employee to teleoperate the robot via VR (source: The Problem with this Humanoid Robot.md).
- **Privacy and Surveillance**: Expert Mode requires inviting remote cameras and microphones into the private home space. While 1X has proposed face-blurring and geofencing limits, early adopters must accept remote human surveillance to generate training data (source: The Problem with this Humanoid Robot.md).

### 4. Fleet-Scale Data Collection Model
- **Parallel to Tesla FSD**: Just as Tesla uses millions of customer vehicles to collect real-world driving data for training self-driving neural networks, 1X plans to use home robots to gather household interaction data (source: The Problem with this Humanoid Robot.md).
- **High-Variable Environments**: Collecting home training data is highly complex due to the vast diversity of layouts, 3D movements, and variations in everyday household objects, compared to standard 2D road navigation (source: The Problem with this Humanoid Robot.md).
- **Safety and Reliability Risks**: Clumsiness, risk of falling, potential to drop fragile items (e.g., glass), and critical safety risks like incorrectly dispensing medication make absolute reliability essential yet difficult to achieve (source: The Problem with this Humanoid Robot.md).
