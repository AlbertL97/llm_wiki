# Auditing Framing-Sensitive Behavioral Instability in Large Language Models for Mental Health Interactions

This paper investigates the impact of contextual framing on the behavior and internal representations of Large Language Models (LLMs), particularly within the sensitive domain of mental health interactions. The research highlights that subtle differences in prompt framing can lead to significant variations in LLM responses, challenging the consistency and trustworthiness of these systems. The study employs controlled experiments, internal probing, and activation steering to demonstrate that framing-related information is encoded within the LLM's architecture and can influence its output, emphasizing the need for robust evaluation methods for AI reliability in psychological applications.

## Key Findings

*   **Framing-Dependent Instability:** LLMs exhibit framing-sensitive behavioral instability, where semantically similar inputs with different contextual framings yield disparate responses.
*   **Internal Representation of Framing:** Analyses show that framing-associated information is decodable throughout the LLM's internal layers, indicating that these models process and represent contextual nuances.
*   **Architectural Variations:** The strength of framing information decoding varies across different LLM architectures.
*   **Robustness Beyond Lexical Cues:** Framing effects persist and are detectable even when lexical baselines are controlled for, suggesting a deeper semantic processing of context.
*   **Modulation of Behavior:** Experimental manipulation of internal representations demonstrates that framing-specific directions can influence the LLM's downstream behavior.
*   **Trustworthiness Implications:** The findings are critical for ensuring the consistency and trustworthiness of conversational AI in mental health applications, where predictable and stable behavior is paramount.
