# Hallucinations in Organization-backed AI Advisors: Evidence about Skepticism, Verification, and Reliance in Goal-Directed Use

This paper examines how users interact with generative AI systems that provide information, particularly when these systems produce inaccurate outputs known as hallucinations. It focuses on understanding user skepticism, verification behaviors, and eventual reliance on information provided by organization-backed AI advisors.

## Key Findings

*   **Distinguishing User Constructs:** The research highlights the importance of differentiating between user skepticism (doubt), verification (checking), and reliance (dependence) when evaluating interactions with AI advisors.
*   **Measurement Gaps:** While reliance on AI information is commonly measured, empirical studies often neglect to directly measure user skepticism and the act of verification itself.
*   **Intervention Effectiveness:** Interventions designed to prompt user scrutiny, such as source citations or explicit warnings about hallucinations, frequently demonstrate weak or mixed effects on user behavior.
*   **Output-Centric Cues:** The primary cues that encourage users to scrutinize AI responses are typically embedded within the AI's output, rather than external factors.
*   **Unanswered Questions:** The impact of content category on user verification behavior is an area requiring further investigation, as current research has not systematically explored this variable.

## Future Research Directions

*   Future research should prioritize measuring user skepticism and verification as distinct constructs from reliance to gain a more accurate understanding of user trust and behavior.
*   Developing and testing more effective strategies for encouraging AI output verification is essential for safe and reliable AI deployment.
