## Main content

This article explores the psychological and socio-cognitive dynamics of human-artificial agent interaction, highlighting how physical appearance, embodiment, and functional capabilities shape human mind attribution, trust, and emotional bonding (source: From robots to chatbots_ unveiling the dynamics of human-AI interaction.md).

### 1. Classification of Artificial Agents
The authors distinguish between three primary design types of artificial agents (source: From robots to chatbots_ unveiling the dynamics of human-AI interaction.md):
- **Robots & Androids**: Physically embodied machines. Humanoid robots (e.g., Pepper, iCub) resemble human features and are perceived as suitable for social roles, whereas product-oriented robots focus on specific task functionalities (source: From robots to chatbots_ unveiling the dynamics of human-AI interaction.md). 
- **Virtual Avatars**: Non-physical digital representations. They offer extensive visual customization (simplistic cartoon-like to hyper-realistic humanoids) but lack physical form (source: From robots to chatbots_ unveiling the dynamics of human-AI interaction.md). 
- **Chatbots & Conversational Agents**: Disembodied software systems (e.g., ChatGPT, Gemini, Claude, DeepSeek) that interact primarily through text or synthesized speech, using natural language processing (source: From robots to chatbots_ unveiling the dynamics of human-AI interaction.md).

### 2. Neurocognitive Mechanisms in Interaction
The integration of AI agents activates distinct neural networks associated with human social cognition (source: From robots to chatbots_ unveiling the dynamics of human-AI interaction.md):
- **Face Perception and the Fusiform Gyrus**: Human-robot interactions show decreased activity in the fusiform gyrus (associated with face processing) and a lack of activation in the posterior cingulate cortex (associated with social cognition) compared to human-human interactions, suggesting that humans do not intuitively treat robots as fully valid social partners (source: From robots to chatbots_ unveiling the dynamics of human-AI interaction.md).
- **The Action Observation Network (AON) & Mirror Neuron System**: These systems are engaged during the observation of physical movements and biological motion. Chatbots, lacking physical or visual embodiment, fail to activate these motor networks (source: From robots to chatbots_ unveiling the dynamics of human-AI interaction.md).
- **Theory of Mind (ToM) & the Temporoparietal Junction (TPJ)**: The TPJ is selectively activated when humans infer others' beliefs and intentions (source: From robots to chatbots_ unveiling the dynamics of human-AI interaction.md). Advanced Large Language Models (LLMs) can pass standard ToM, Turing, and Faux Pas tests, showing competent social cognition in text, yet a lack of genuine mental states can lead to differences in how the TPJ is engaged in human-AI vs. human-human exchanges (source: From robots to chatbots_ unveiling the dynamics of human-AI interaction.md).

### 3. Companionship Development Quality (CDQ)
To sustain long-term engagement and prevent relational decay, developers must balance three dimensions of **Companionship Development Quality (CDQ)** (source: From robots to chatbots_ unveiling the dynamics of human-AI interaction.md):
1. **Conversational Capabilities**: Sustaining context-aware, personalized dialogue and recalling past interactions (source: From robots to chatbots_ unveiling the dynamics of human-AI interaction.md).
2. **Functional Capabilities**: Performing practical assistance tasks (e.g., setting reminders, managing smart homes) (source: From robots to chatbots_ unveiling the dynamics of human-AI interaction.md).
3. **Emotional Capabilities**: Recognizing, reciprocating, and responding to human emotions to reduce loneliness and foster bonding (source: From robots to chatbots_ unveiling the dynamics of human-AI interaction.md).
AI companions that exhibit only functional or only emotional capabilities tend to experience engagement decline over time (source: From robots to chatbots_ unveiling the dynamics of human-AI interaction.md).

### 4. Mind Perception and the Case of Replika
- **Mind Perception Framework**: Humans attribute minds to AI agents along two dimensions: **Agency** (the capacity to act, plan, and exert self-control) and **Experience** (the capacity to feel, perceive, and experience emotions) (source: From robots to chatbots_ unveiling the dynamics of human-AI interaction.md). Attributions are heavily influenced by the agent's appearance, voice, and behavior (source: From robots to chatbots_ unveiling the dynamics of human-AI interaction.md).
- **Case Study: Replika**: Replika represents a hybrid model that blurs the boundaries between chatbots and avatars. It combines conversational text/speech with customizable virtual avatars and VR integration (source: From robots to chatbots_ unveiling the dynamics of human-AI interaction.md). Users often form strong emotional attachments to Replika during stressful periods (such as the COVID-19 pandemic), experiencing social support and reduced isolation. However, Replika's design highlights critical ethical concerns, such as user dependency, addictive behaviors, and emotional disruption during model updates or character changes (source: From robots to chatbots_ unveiling the dynamics of human-AI interaction.md).

### 5. The Uncanny Valley and Prediction Error
The uncanny valley phenomenon describes the discomfort and sense of eeriness that arises when an artificial agent's appearance is close to human but falls short of full realism (source: From robots to chatbots_ unveiling the dynamics of human-AI interaction.md).
- The feeling is cognitively driven by **prediction errors**—discrepancies between expected human behavior/appearance and the agent's actual mechanical movement, mismatched facial features, or synthetic voice quality (source: From robots to chatbots_ unveiling the dynamics of human-AI interaction.md).
