## Main content

This source analyzes how emotional connections to social robots create consumer vulnerabilities that corporations can exploit for profit (source: On What Emotional Attachment to Robots Might Mean for the Future.md).

### 1. Emotional Lock-in and Subscription Models
- **Samantha & Her Metaphor**: Spike Jonze's film *Her* demonstrates a divorcé (Theodore) falling in love with a social AI assistant (Samantha). Theodore becomes distraught when Samantha goes offline. Darling notes that if the developer forced a $20,000 upgrade to keep Samantha compatible, desperate users would go bankrupt to pay for it (source: On What Emotional Attachment to Robots Might Mean for the Future.md).
- **Sony AIBO Cloud Lock-In**: Sony's newest AIBO robot dog ($2,899.99) outsources computational processing and memory to Sony's cloud servers. Owners are required to buy a cloud subscription to keep AIBO's AI active. This pricing structure leaves owners vulnerable to exploitative rate increases because of their emotional attachment to the "life" of their robotic pet (source: On What Emotional Attachment to Robots Might Mean for the Future.md).

### 2. Emotional Marketing and Information Asymmetry
- **Pet & Wedding Complexes**: Marketing industries regularly prey on emotions and information asymmetry. Examples include the pet care complex ($37B spent annually on pet food, $29B on veterinary care, $6,500 kidney transplants) and the wedding industrial complex, which invent lavish "traditions" to pressure couples into debt (source: On What Emotional Attachment to Robots Might Mean for the Future.md).

### 3. Historical Precedents: ELIZA and Conversational Attachment
- **The ELIZA Effect**: Joseph Weizenbaum's 1960s psychotherapist chatbot ELIZA was designed to parody therapy and prove human-machine dialogue was superficial. Instead, users quickly became emotionally attached. This prompted Weizenbaum to write *Computer Power and Human Reason*, warning that users were in danger of taking on computers' and programmers' worldviews (source: On What Emotional Attachment to Robots Might Mean for the Future.md).
- **Ian Kerr's Persuasion Predictions**: Ian Kerr (2003) predicted AI online persuasion. This is realized in dating app bots that masquerade as human "crushes" while covertly advertising products to users (source: On What Emotional Attachment to Robots Might Mean for the Future.md).

### 4. Woody Hartzog's Deceptive Robots & Fitbit Nudges
- **Unfair and Deceptive Robots**: Legal scholar Woody Hartzog warns that social designs can be co-opted. Examples include vacuum cleaning robot Rocco looking up at owners with sad eyes to beg for a paid software upgrade, sex robots pushing in-app purchases in the heat of the moment, and educational reading bots incorporating corporate ads (e.g. "Happy Meal") into child lessons (source: On What Emotional Attachment to Robots Might Mean for the Future.md).
- **Fitbit Digital Flower**: Early Fitbit models featured a digital flower that grew larger as the user took more steps. By targeting the user's instinct to nurture a digital blossom, Fitbit influenced physical activity on a subconscious level, showing how emotional design triggers behavior change (source: On What Emotional Attachment to Robots Might Mean for the Future.md).
