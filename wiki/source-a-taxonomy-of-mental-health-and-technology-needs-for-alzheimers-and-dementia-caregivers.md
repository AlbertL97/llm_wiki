# A Taxonomy of Mental Health and Technology Needs for Alzheimer's and Dementia Caregivers

## Overview
This article introduces a novel Caregiver Mental Health and Technology Taxonomy, designed to systematically connect the diverse and complex psychosocial needs of family caregivers for individuals with Alzheimer's disease and related dementias (AD/ADRD) with appropriate digital and AI-enabled technological interventions. By drawing on an interdisciplinary literature review and qualitative studies with caregivers, the research aims to overcome the limitations of simplifying caregiver experiences to a single construct of 'burden,' thereby enabling more targeted and effective technological support for their mental health.

## Key Findings and Contributions
*   **Caregiver Mental Health and Technology Taxonomy:** The core contribution is a new framework that systematically maps AD/ADRD caregiver mental health needs to technology-based interventions.
*   **Identification of Unmet Needs:** The study uncovers significant discrepancies between what caregivers prioritize for support and what existing technologies, including AI chatbots and sensor platforms, currently offer.
*   **Highlighting Under-served Domains:** It draws attention to specific, critical mental health challenges often overlooked by current technological solutions, such as relational strain and compassion fatigue among caregivers.
*   **Design Directions for Adaptive Systems:** The research proposes actionable design principles for developing more adaptive, responsive, and person-centered AI systems and other digital technologies tailored to the dynamic needs of dementia caregivers.
*   **Shared Vocabulary:** The taxonomy provides a common language for clinicians, researchers, and technology designers to foster clinically grounded and innovative solutions in dementia care.

## Methodology
The taxonomy was developed through a robust methodology comprising an interdisciplinary literature review and two qualitative studies conducted directly with AD/ADRD caregivers. This mixed-methods approach ensured both breadth of theoretical understanding and depth of lived experience.

## Implications for Human-AI/Robot Interaction Psychology
This work is highly relevant to Human-AI Interaction Psychology, particularly in the context of mental health support. It offers critical insights into:
*   **Designing AI for Vulnerable Populations:** Provides a framework for understanding and addressing the complex mental health needs of caregivers, informing the design of AI companions or support systems (e.g., chatbots) for individuals facing chronic stress and emotional challenges.
*   **Person-Centered AI:** Emphasizes the importance of adaptive and responsive AI systems that are sensitive to individual caregiver priorities and nuanced psychosocial experiences, moving beyond one-size-fits-all solutions.
*   **Bridging Research Gaps:** Creates a framework that can guide future research and development at the intersection of psychology, medicine, and AI technology, particularly concerning the ethical and effective deployment of AI in healthcare.
